# Chapter 11: Wind in the Alley

Derek called while Ethan was working on a knee joint. Hands oily.

“Come drink tonight.”

“When.”

“Tonight.”

A barbecue joint at the end of a dead-end street. No sign outside – just smoke coming from a steel smoker by the back door, the lid cracked open, heat shimmering above it. Screen door – wood frame, the spring loose – pushed through with a hand.

Inside, three picnic tables with brown paper taped to the surfaces, already translucent with grease in spots. Wooden benches. Low light – a string of bare bulbs overhead, one out. A neon beer sign by the register, the N flickering.

Ben was there too. Thinner. Hair longer – over his ears, resting on his collar. Sat down, poured himself a beer, didn’t say anything, drank. His Adam’s apple moved twice. Glass empty.

“My SaaS – a better model came out. Overnight. Free.”

“Two years,” Derek said. “Two years.”

“You?” Ethan asked Ben.

Ben wasn’t at the repair station. Still looking.

“You? The repair station okay?” Derek looked at Ethan.

“It’s okay.”

“How long can you do it?”

“Don’t know.”

“Last month at that tech conference. The AI company itself laid off a thousand people. The people who make AI, replaced by AI.” Derek laughed. Nothing in the laugh.

Ribs arrived. And corn on the cob and slaw and jalapeño poppers. Hickory smell. Smoke drifting between them from the pit – eyes stung – blinked.

Derek took a rib. Bit. Chewed.

“You remember freshman year?”

“What?”

“Thirty bucks. That poster.”

“Yeah.”

“Thirty bucks.” Laughed again. He set the bare bone on the table – parallel to another one – then another – three parallel bones.

“Thirty bucks. Now I spend two grand a month on AI. I don’t know what I’m buying. But I pay.”

The alley. Stepped outside for a moment. Night. Wind. The hair on his arms stood up.

The alley narrow. Old walls on both sides. Brick. Mortar crumbling in places – exposing gaps – like mouths – empty. Moss at the base. Green. Damp.

A cat walked from the far end of the alley. Gray. Looked at them once. Kept walking.

“Any regrets?” Derek said.

“About what?”

“College. Your major.”

“Don’t know.” He leaned against the wall. The brick surface cold, rough. Through his shirt he could feel the texture. “You?”

“Me?” Derek took a drag. The cigarette tip glowed in the dark – orange – dimmed. “I regret that everything’s too late. Everything is too late.”

“What’s too late?”

No answer. Smoke came from his mouth. Went up. Dissipated.

Finished the cigarette. Stepped on it. A black mark on the ground. “Let’s go back in.”

Sat back down. Opened another bottle.

Ben: “I bet on AI winning. AI won. But I lost.”

“Retirement money – my parents’ – it’s in there too.” He poured another glass. Didn’t drink. Looked at the beer. Clear. Bubbles on the glass wall. “The fund manager auto-allocated to AI stocks. Everyone’s retirement is in there. My parents’. Your parents’.”

Nobody spoke. The smoker had gone quiet outside.

Derek’s phone lit up. The table buzzed. He picked it up. Screen: Enterprise tier renewal notice. $1,999.00. Thumb swipe. Dark. Set it down. Picked up his glass.

A while later Ethan’s phone lit up too. Basic income deposit. Short number. A crack in the screen’s upper right corner – from when he’d dropped it – the crack ran through the last digit of the amount. Pressed the button. Dark.

Three phones on the table. Derek’s – $1,999.00. Ethan’s – basic income. Ben’s – the fund page, green curve. Three phones. Three kinds of numbers. Same table. The smoker’s residual heat drifting in – the table surface warm – three phones on the warm surface.

Ben stood up. “Bathroom.”

His phone was face-down on the table. He walked off.

Ethan wasn’t trying to look. His eyes caught it. Ben’s phone had shifted – the screen lit for an instant – the fund page – the green curve – the name at the top –

Dark.

“Nobody’s fault,” Derek said. He stared at the three phones on the table. Opened his mouth, then closed it. The base of his glass knocked the table. Spilled a little – in the lamplight it gleamed – from the glass base a small trail ran toward the table edge – stopped. Didn’t fall.

More time passed. Derek had drunk too much. He picked up his phone. A push notification. Investment advice. AI-generated.

Looked at it two seconds. Tapped.

“AI says buy this. I buy. Makes money every time. But I can’t understand why it decides what it decides anymore. I ask it. It explains. Very clearly, very patiently. But the explanation –”

Stopped.

“It’s like – an adult answering a kid. The more it explains the simpler it gets. By the end you know every word. You still don’t understand.”

Stopped. Glass on the table, his hand unsteady. Spilled.

“It’s like –” he was thinking – his hand drew something on the table surface – “humoring a kid –”

Stopped.

“You press this button.”

Stopped.

“The elevator was going to arrive anyway.”

He startled himself with that sentence. His eyes shifted.

“Then who’s operating the elevator,” Ethan said.

Nobody answered. The foam at the bottom of the glass had dissolved.

Derek’s phone rang again.

“My eldercare business is pretty much done. Home care robots. Small ones. Live in the old person’s house. Can cook. Can help them up. Can take blood pressure. They fall at night, it catches them and calls 911. Cheaper than a caregiver. Twenty-four-seven. Won’t steal. Won’t lose patience.”

He took another drink. “The families feel better. A robot won’t hurt grandma.”

His tongue was thick now. “How am I any different from you clicking Approved back then.”

He slumped on the table. Ben too.

The rain had started.

On the table surface someone had drawn a few lines with a ballpoint pen. Blue.

Not blue. That pen was green.

Ethan sat across from them. Not sleeping.

His phone. His father calling.

“Your mom asked me to call. Where are you?”

“Out. Eating.”

“Mm.” Pause. Background: the TV – news – the anchor’s voice – steady. “Eating what?”

“BBQ.”

“Mm. Don’t drink too much.”

Another pause. The couch leather creaked – shifted position.

“That fund – I looked at it – it’s up quite a bit.”

“Yeah.”

“I know which companies those are.”

Ethan said nothing.

“The names I recognize. They’re on the news every day.” Pause. “Your mom doesn’t know. I didn’t tell her. She doesn’t follow that stuff. I – don’t really either. But the names I recognize.”

Outside, rain sound. The screen door rattling in the wind.

“What went up went up. Don’t worry about it. The gains from those – I sent to you. Last week. Check your account.”

“Dad –”

“My choice.” The voice changed – not softer – lower – half a note – like something being set down.

Outside, rain on the alley. The sound of it on the smoker’s lid. Tin.

Hung up.

Call duration: one minute, forty-two seconds. His father’s name on the screen, lit one second. Dark.

Derek was slumped over. His back rising and falling. Breathing had evened. Asleep.

Derek woke up. Called a ride.

Stepped outside. The alley. Rain.

Took two steps. Bent over. Threw up.

Ethan stood beside him. He was bent at the waist. His back – shirt sticking – wet –

Ethan put a hand on his shoulder.

The shoulder – bone beneath the fabric – round – hard – the acromion pressing up against his palm through the cloth. Rain hitting his back. The shirt plastered along both sides of the spine. The same way his father’s shirt had stuck to his back at the train platform years ago.

The car left. Taillights in the rain. Red. Blurred. Turned the corner. Gone.

Ben was still in the alley. Leaning against the wall. The rain falling on him. Not sheltering.

“Go inside.”

“One second.”

He stood in the rain. Face tilted up. Rain on his face. Eyes closed.

Waited a minute. Lowered his head. Wiped his face. “Let’s go.”
