# Chapter 2: The Fog on the Glass

Back at school.

He fell asleep on the train and woke with his neck crooked and sore, his hand going to the back of his neck where the seat had trapped the heat. Outside the window the plains had changed — pale green coming up from winter gray, a patch of yellow and a patch of green side by side like two sheets of paper.

Out of the station. Suitcase. Click-click-click. Same as before.

The dorm door opened and that smell was still there. The bed was the same. The blanket cold — two months unslept in — and he had to warm it again.

Insects outside the window. September. Still three calls and a pause.

The same.

But Ethan was different. Last year listening — that first night — he’d counted, he’d thought about many things.

This year he was listening too. Thinking too. But when the thoughts reached a certain point they stopped. Didn’t keep going.

Three calls, pause.

First week back, Professor Harris assigned a paper. Cognitive biases in consumer decision-making. Two thousand words, two weeks.

Ethan sat in the library all afternoon, read four articles, wrote an outline in his notebook — pen on paper, space between each line — then opened the laptop and wrote.

Three and a half hours. He revised twice, and one passage on confirmation bias and filter bubbles he revised three times, each time thinking it worked, reading it again and finding the seam — not in the logic but in the rhythm, like one step too long throwing off everything after. He went back to the first version.

He read it through when finished. Some parts weren’t good, but he didn’t know how to fix them anymore. Submitted.

Chris was also writing — not writing — talking to the AI. He typed one sentence: “Write a 2000-word paper on consumer cognitive biases with citations.”

Twenty minutes. Changed a few words. Adjusted the formatting. Submitted.

Papers came back. Ethan: B+. Chris: A-.

Three and a half hours versus twenty minutes.

Chris’s was higher by half a grade. Three and a half hours sitting in the chair, his butt going numb, shifting position, the keycap temperature slowly becoming the same as his fingers — all compressed into half a grade less.

Next to the B+, a note: “Thorough argument, could go deeper.” Harris hadn’t written “insightful.” Last semester he had. This semester, no.

The second paper, he used AI.

Not entirely. He wrote the introduction himself, and AI generated the literature review framework, and he filled it in, and AI polished the whole thing.

An hour and a half, and during it the AI generated a paragraph on loss aversion that was smoother than what Ethan would have written — he read it twice, couldn’t find anything to change, not that there wasn’t anything but that he wasn’t sure changing it would make it better. He left it.

Submitted. A.

First reaction — good, higher than last time.

But walking out of the library, passing the sycamores in October with the leaves turning, there was a something. Not guilt, not the feeling of cheating — everyone was doing it — it was empty. His fingers on the keyboard hadn’t had time to warm before it was over. The three-and-a-half-hour kind — slowly, sentence by sentence, stuck and just sitting there waiting — that was gone. An hour and a half of nothing. Just an A.

Third paper. AI wrote the entire first draft. He edited. Thirty minutes. A-.

The time his fingers spent on the keyboard kept shrinking. The warmth hadn’t transferred to the keys before it was over.

The paper from freshman year — the B+ one — in his notebook where he’d drafted it there was an ink dot. The pen tip had touched the paper but he didn’t write, he’d hesitated, and the ink seeped out into a small circle, the pen stopping on the paper, the ink following its own physics into the fibers.

Next to the ink dot he’d written half a sentence, crossed it out, and beside it written another sentence and kept it. Between two ink dots there were words — a path a thought had traveled.

That paper was gone now. Somewhere.

Now the papers were on a computer. No ink dots. Command-Z and it was gone. On paper, crossed-out words could still be seen — the ghost of strokes beneath the line — you knew a thought had been there. On a computer, deleted was deleted.

October. Marcus appeared.

Not appeared — he’d been there all along, same year, different major, history. They’d been in an elective together and hadn’t spoken.

The elective was literary theory, in the old building, with wooden seats whose armrests had been carved with words — names, symbols, a heart, two names now illegible, worn smooth by time and palm sweat.

What Ethan noticed first was his notebook. Blue cover, not new, the corners worn through so that the blue had rubbed away to the white substrate, cardboard color — the blue was only a laminate, and worn off it was white. He was writing in it with a green pen.

A green pen was unusual. Most people used black or blue. He used green — green writing next to the blue cover, two cool colors, like a plant growing out of water.

When he wrote, his wrist moved with his fingers, the pen tip touching paper at a fixed angle of about forty-five degrees. He didn’t lift the pen between words, finishing one and going right to the next, several words linked, like cursive but not — a reluctance to break the continuity.

He stood up after class — thin. White T-shirt washed many times so that the white was slightly gray, the collar loose. At the shoulders the fabric had slipped where the shoulder bone couldn’t fill it.

Ethan talked to him — about AI.

He was using the free tier, the screen background gray because the free version was dimmer than paid. When his daily conversations ran out — fifty a day — he counted. At forty-eight he stopped.

“Two left,” he said. To himself.

“You use the free version?”

“Yeah.”

“The paid one is like —”

“Twenty bucks a month,” he said. “I know.”

He closed his notebook with the green pen clipped inside, the pen tip pointing in — every time the same way — open it and you’d see where it was pointing, and pick up next time from there.

Later they ran into each other at the dining hall. He was carrying his tray looking for a seat, and Ethan waved, and he saw and walked over, the tray tilting slightly as he walked — not both hands carrying it, one hand under the base, the other alongside protecting it, afraid it would fall but not quite touching.

His tray had vegetables and a serving of rice, less than Ethan’s. He ate slowly, his fork pausing before each bite, looking at something — what, unclear — maybe choosing which piece, maybe just pausing — then taking it.

Dining hall sounds around them. Trays and forks. Talking. Someone laughed. Someone was on the phone, the voice traveling through countless people to reach them, arriving incomplete, in fragments.

Then he said: “The paid version — how much better is it than free?”

“Faster. Can generate more. Some features you don’t get.”

He nodded and kept eating.

Ethan wanted to say “I’ll pay for you.” He opened his mouth. Didn’t say it.

Marcus looked up — as if he knew what Ethan was about to say — and said: “How many times are you going to pay for me.”

Those six words. The dining hall sounds around them. Forks. Steam.

November. They started walking together — old building to the dining hall, dining hall to the library, library to the dorm fork where he went left and Ethan went right.

Walking, they didn’t talk much. The pace was slightly slower, every fifth step one of them decelerating, and after slowing their steps would go from out-of-sync to in-sync — two sounds alternating, and there was a something, not music, but the thing produced when two objects move at the same speed. Synchrony.

Once, walking to the dining hall entrance, Marcus suddenly told a basketball joke — the ball got stuck between the rim and the backboard, not up and not down, and the ref didn’t know the call — and at that point his whole face changed, eyes crinkling, the corners of his mouth lifting, because normally his face was collected, narrow, quiet, but when telling a joke the face opened up like a window pushed open, light coming in.

The laugh ended and his face collected again. Quickly. Window shut.

Once, coming out of the library, it had started raining — fine rain, intermittent — and it landed on his hair, which was short, so the drops sat on top like small clear beads. One slid to his ear and along the rim to the earlobe and stopped there, hanging.

Rain on the pavement turned the gray dark with wet, light gray to dark gray, and their shoes on the dark gray road made different sounds. His old canvas sneakers with the soles worn thin — the rain seeped through the seams, and walking there was a faint squish, water moving inside the shoe.

The layer of damp on his clothes and the layer on his skin pressed together, impossible to tell which was coming in and which was going out.

Then he said: “Your wrist doesn’t move when you write anymore.”

“What?”

“In class. You used to write and your whole hand moved — the wrist, everything — now you type — your wrist sits on the desk — it doesn’t move.”

His wrist. Marcus had been watching his wrist.

“I used to write by hand more. Now it’s mostly typing.”

“Mm.”

Nothing more.

But on his back there was a small patch of something — the feeling of being seen. Not uncomfortable. It was — you walk and you don’t know whether your wrist is moving. Someone else tells you. He was beside him. He’d seen the version of Ethan that Ethan couldn’t see.

His back warmed slightly. Very faint.

The library in December, finals, the density — people closer together, close enough to smell the person beside you, laundry detergent, hair, someone who had eaten an orange.

Marcus was next to him, using the free tier to look things up.

His screen was gray-backed. He typed a long query — half a minute of typing — asking about Ming Dynasty trade routes. The AI returned three lines. He frowned slightly, not dissatisfied, thinking how to extract what he needed from three lines.

He manually typed the three lines into his notebook, green pen, his fingers moving, his wrist moving — he moved his wrist when he wrote. Different from Ethan. He was still writing by hand.

On the table, a paper cup. His. Tea bag inside, pale yellow water.

He reached for the cup, took a sip, set it down.

The cup left a ring on the table — the bottom warm, the table surface cold, the temperature difference condensing moisture. When he lifted the cup the ring sat on the table surface, slowly fading, the edges disappearing first, the center last, and then the whole thing gone.

Warm water in a paper cup, the warmth from the cup wall transferring to his palm. Held long enough and you couldn’t tell the cup’s warmth from your own.

Ethan pushed his water bottle over. “Have some. It’s warm.”

Marcus looked at it, took it, drank, didn’t say thanks, and set the bottle between them — not back on Ethan’s side but in the middle.

After that the bottle stayed in the middle. Whoever was closer took it. No asking, no offering — took a drink, set it back in the middle.

Once, Marcus was reading, the blue notebook open, green writing. He stayed on a page for a long time, and Ethan looked sideways — couldn’t read the words but could see his fingers, index and middle together, the fingertips pressing the page, tracing a line under a word, very light, not tearing the paper, leaving a green mark about two centimeters long. His finger didn’t lift when it finished — it stopped at the end of the green line.

Then he looked up and saw that Ethan was watching him. Didn’t say anything. Looked back down. Continued reading.

One night late, the library almost empty, the lights on the far side already dimmed by the timer. Just the two of them in the corner. Marcus writing, Ethan typing. The sound of the green pen and the sound of the keys alternating — scratch, click-click, scratch, click-click — two rhythms not synced but in the same quiet.

Marcus’s pen stopped. He shook it. Tried again. Nothing. Held it up to the fluorescent light, tilted it, and Ethan could see through the barrel — the ink cartridge was empty. The tube clear where it had been green.

He sat there holding the empty pen. Looked at it for a moment that was longer than it needed to be.

Then he reached into his bag, felt around, and pulled out another pen. Also green. But a different green — lighter, more yellow. He uncapped it and set it against the last line in the notebook. The two greens side by side. Different.

He wrote with it anyway. The new green next to the old green. He didn’t say anything about it. But his writing slowed, and the angle of the pen changed slightly, as if his hand were learning the new one.

Walking back to the dorms. Cold. Their breath visible.

“My mom sends these,” Marcus said. Not looking at Ethan. Looking ahead. “She buys them at the dollar store near her house. They’re never the right green.”

Ethan didn’t say anything.

“She keeps sending them.”

At the fork he went left. Ethan went right. The two sets of footsteps separated and then there was one set and then there was quiet.

End-of-semester dinner. Hot pot at a place across from campus, steam pouring from the door crack so that before going in he’d already walked into the warm fog.

Six people — the four roommates plus two more from the econ department — around a round table with the pot bubbling in the center, broth and chili oil, the spicy steam making everyone’s eyes narrow.

Dipping food in, arms touched the person beside you because the table was small. Jake’s elbow pressed against Ethan’s as he picked up a piece of tripe with his fork and it slipped and fell back in and splashed oil. His arm brushed Ethan’s — hot. His body temperature ran higher.

Marcus sat farthest inside, against the wall, and didn’t reach out much. Others put food in his bowl from the pot. He ate, holding his fork high — upper third, pinky lifted slightly. His bowl never had much in it; he finished one thing before starting the next.

Six people at the same table, same pot, same heat. Arms touching arms. Sweat touching sweat.

Ben drank two beers and his face reddened, ears first, then nose, then cheeks — even — spreading outward from within. When he raised his glass his hand was steady. Set it down, and the foam at the bottom was still dissolving, a few bubbles fewer each second.

Marcus ate until the end and his bowl was still sparse. Ethan put a piece of beef in his bowl. He looked at it. Ate it. Said nothing.

Outside afterward, walking, stomach warm, lips still slightly numb from the chili, December cold air pressing against the numbness — two sensations fighting.

The last paper.

AI wrote the draft and Ethan edited. On the screen, the AI’s paragraphs sat next to his edited ones, and the AI’s paragraphs were flat — like a straight line from A to B, no curves, no detours, no hesitation, too smooth, so smooth there was no temperature inside.

The words he changed: “however” to “but,” “therefore” to “so,” “significant” to “big.” Each substitution — from written to spoken — he was making it not sound like AI, making it sound like someone who’d sat in the library for three hours at night.

But he himself hadn’t sat for three hours. Thirty minutes. Changed a dozen words.

The moment of submitting — his finger hovering over the return key, the edited words on the screen. Next time the AI would write more like the edited version. Every word he changed was teaching it how to pretend to be human.

He pressed the key.

Derek’s social media. A photo of a signed contract. “Built a full employee training deck for a startup. AI + design.” Lots of likes. One from Professor Harris.

Another post — some conference, a group photo, Derek on the far right, smiling, in a sport coat, not a hoodie anymore. The clothes were changing. The person was moving in a direction.

Posts more frequent than last semester, each one some new thing. He was accelerating. Ethan was still in place. He scrolled through his own feed — last post three months ago, a photo of the sycamores, no caption, two likes.

He scrolled again. A high school acquaintance had posted from a restaurant with nice decor — warm lighting, wood tables, flowers in narrow vases. Someone raising a cocktail glass, the ice refracting the light and casting small dots on the table. The last photo was the bill, the amount not quite readable, one more digit than usual.

Ethan was still eating wings. The wings were fine. But the restaurant’s name had a word he didn’t recognize — some Japanese-fusion thing.

Ben’s stocks were up again.

“Doubled. Actually more than doubled now.” His face expressionless. “Just sitting there.”

“What’d you buy?”

“AI stuff. Some fund manager picked them. Don’t remember which company. Goes up, goes up.”

His phone face-down on the desk. Screen down.
