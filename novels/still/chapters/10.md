# Chapter 10: Steel Wool

Coveralls. Blue. Pulled them on – the fabric stiff – new – washed but not yet worn soft – the collar rubbing his neck – sleeves too long – had to roll them. Steel-toed boots. Heavy. Space in front of his toes – the steel cap ahead of him – kick something and it wouldn’t hurt. But his feet were heavier. Walking sounded different – the tap became a thud.

The repair station was at the back of an industrial park. A sheet-metal building. Gray-green. A tree by the door – small leaves – dense – the whole tree trembling in the wind. The door was metal. Pulling it open, the track scraped the ground – a screech – heavy.

Inside. Gray. Concrete floor. Tools on the wall – wrenches, pliers, drill, screwdrivers – each on its own hook – hanging – like a row of arms reaching out.

Frank. Fifties. Used to fix cars. Didn’t talk much. Lines in his face deepest at the eyes – not smile lines – squint lines from looking at things closely. Making tea. Enamel mug. White with faded red flowers. Most of the color gone. A chip at the rim – porcelain fallen away – iron beneath – rusted – when he drank he moved his lips around the chip – he knew where it was.

“Mornin’.”

“Morning.”

“Today – warehouse robot. Right shoulder joint. Bearing’s shot.”

The bearing. Opened the packaging. New. Twelve steel balls in a ring. Each one bright as a mirror – the light’s reflection – a small white dot – on each one – twelve small white dots.

The old bearing pulled out. The steel balls had wear marks – not round anymore – oblong – skewed – and skewed they couldn’t spin smoothly – the unsmoothness made sound – sound was the wear speaking.

New bearing installed. Last two turns by hand. Power tools on the last two turns can strip the threads – Frank had taught him. Fingers on the wrench end, the wrist making a small arc – not large – the minimum arc – each turn the gap tightened – the sound changed – from loose clicks to tight clicks –

Last half-turn went stiff. His knuckles whitened. Couldn’t turn it more. Stopped.

Crouched under the chassis checking wiring. Knees on concrete. Cold. The cold traveled up from his knees. Reached his thighs and stopped – the pants blocked it. A loose connector. Finger pushed the plug in – click – his finger felt something catch. Right.

Two hours. Back aching. Wrist aching. Crouching, his pant knees had picked up gray dust. Standing – his knee popped.

Frank passed him tea. The enamel mug. The red flower only half remaining. Tea was strong. Bitter. Swallowing, his mouth held an aftertaste – not sweet – the space left after bitterness departed.

“Good hands,” Frank said. He didn’t say that often.

One day Frank didn’t come in. Sick.

Ethan opened the door alone. The sheet-metal building was quiet. Tools on the wall. Without Frank the tools looked bigger – the wrench hanging there – normally it was a thing in Frank’s hand – without Frank it was just a wrench – cold – unrelated to anyone.

One work order. Warehouse robot. Left knee joint loose.

Worked alone. Crouched. Opened the housing. The structure was familiar now – fourth unit of the same model – wiring layout memorized – red left, yellow center, blue right. Loosened two bolts. Wrench. Turned. Last two turns by hand.

His fingers felt every groove of the threads. The metal grooves and his fingerprint ridges aligned – like two languages – incompatible – but pressed together on the same plane. Last turn – resistance – stopped.

Stood up. Test run. Joint turned. Sound normal.

One person in the sheet-metal building. Tools on the wall. The robot in front of him. Outside, light came through the gaps in the sheet metal – thin stripes – falling on the concrete floor. Dust floating in the light.

Washing hands. Steel wool.

Water cold. Tasting of iron.

Scrubbed across his palm. It scraped the palm lines. A tiny wire snagged. His palm reddened. Pulled back. Kept scrubbing.

The grease washed off. Under the nails, permanently dark.

His hands under the cold water. Water flowing between his fingers. The fingertips had roughened. Every line of the print visible.

Second unit. A sorting arm. Left wrist making noise.

Opened the housing and smelled it – copper dust and a faint scorch. Frank sniffed once. “Gear oil needs changing.” The way he said it was a little like Ethan’s father. Looked and knew.

Old oil flowing from the gear was black. Dripping on cardboard, it spread from black to gray. New oil was clear with a tint of amber. Poured on, the gear teeth gleamed – each tooth’s side face had wear marks – like miniature terraces – step after step – the cut surfaces bright – the uncut dark.

After the change, test run. Hum. Even. No noise. Frank watched it turn twice. “That’ll do.”

Ethan also watched it turn twice. His hands had oil. In every fingerprint line, dark. Washed, slightly lighter. Not fully clean. The lines too deep. Oil had seeped in. The same kind as the yellow on his father’s hands. Not stuck on. Grown in.

Temperature had come back. But not the same. He could feel. The roughness of the steel wool. The cold of the wrench. The resistance of the last two turns. Every day he felt these things. But he couldn’t remember the last time he’d felt another person’s body heat.

The new apprentice arrived. Good school. Used to do brand strategy. Thin metal glasses frames. Thin lenses. Wearing the coveralls – didn’t look like he was wearing coveralls – looked like he was wearing someone else’s clothes. Rolling up the sleeves.

“Two hundred people. Cut to fifty. Cut to twenty. Cut to three. Cut to one. That one person’s salary went up to what the CEO used to make.” He said this holding a wrench. Too heavy. Switched hands.

“Seven years in branding. Last year the AI redid the whole thing in two weeks. Client said about the same.”

He was learning to clean bearings. His hand was shaking. Not cold. Unfamiliar. A steel ball slipped from the bearing seat – caught it with a cloth – wiped each one – slow – careful. Oil marks on the cloth. His fingerprints were starting to show dark. Hand still shaking. But wiping.

Professor Harris retired. He heard about it. The philosophy department had been gutted. His finger drawing circles in the air – drawing a circle – stopping there – nothing inside – but he believed there was something –

Now those circles were gone. The air didn’t hold his circles anymore.

Frank was looking at a new model robot. Its self-diagnostic system had been upgraded.

“Give this one two more years, might not need us either.”

The tone: like talking about the weather.

The company that built it was worth billions. The people who fixed it made a few thousand a month.

Ethan didn’t think about this. He was turning a bolt. Wrist arc. Wrench weight. Metal touching metal.

His hands were doing what they knew. They didn’t need his brain to tell them. His hands knew. His hands remembered every bolt’s tension. His hands remembered what each sound meant – loose or tight – right or wrong. His brain could think about nothing. His hands were doing.

When he signed reports, his hand also bent. But after bending, the hand was empty.

Turning bolts was different. His hand knew what it was turning. His fingers felt the metal rotating – threads engaging – each degree of rotation the resistance changed – the change was information – traveling from metal to finger to wrist.

Things his hands knew.
