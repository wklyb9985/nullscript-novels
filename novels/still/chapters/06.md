# Chapter 6: 97.3

The diploma was lighter than he’d expected. Red cover. He opened it. A piece of paper.

Six weeks of searching. Over sixty applications sent. Most didn’t reply. A few automated responses. “Thank you for your application, we will review it shortly.” All the same words.

AI Output Review Specialist.

Job description: Review reports generated by AI system. Sign off upon confirmation of accuracy.

“AI system comprehensive accuracy rate: 97.3%.”

97.3. The decimal point like a mole.

First day. The building was on the west side of downtown. Glass and steel. Twelve floors. The lobby had a screen showing a loop. The company logo, then a line: “Precision. Efficiency. Trust.” Then the logo again.

The elevator. Seventh floor. The hallway smelled of carpet cleaner and something else. Toner. Warm plastic. The smell of machines that had been running all morning. At the end of the hall, a window. Through it, the tops of buildings, a water tower, a crane in the distance turning slowly. A pigeon on the sill outside. It shifted its weight from one foot to the other.

His workstation. An old computer. The keyboard still had the previous person’s hand-sweat marks. Spacebar brightest. The F and J bumps worn smooth. The chair was the wrong height. Adjusted twice. Still wrong. Sat in it.

On the desk, a pen. Black. Standard issue. He picked it up. The grip section had a ridge of plastic. The mold seam. His thumb pressed onto it. Signed his name on a Post-it to test the ink. The arc of his signature. Same as always.

A woman came to his workstation. Short hair. Badge clipped to her collar. It read “Training.” She stood beside his chair and pointed at the screen.

“This is the queue. Each report has a header, a body, and a summary. AI generates all three. You read. If it looks right, click Approve. If something looks off, click Reject and note the reason.”

Her finger tapped the screen once. On Approve. The nail made a small click.

“Forty-five seconds per report. The system tracks your average.”

She left. He was looking at the screen. One report. White background. Black text. Footer in gray: “This report was generated by an AI system and takes effect upon human review and approval.”

Approved. Signed. Next.

Beside him. One desk over. A man. About Ethan’s age. Maybe a year or two older. Tom. Round face. A paper coffee cup on his desk. The sleeve had been torn off and folded into a small square, sitting next to the cup like a coaster nobody asked for. He signed reports with his left hand. The pen tilted at an angle different from Ethan’s. The ink line thicker on one side.

When Tom’s screen refreshed he’d lean back in his chair and the backrest would creak. A high sound. Once. Then he’d lean forward again.

He didn’t talk much. Neither did Ethan. But at around eleven every morning Tom would stand, stretch. Arms overhead, wrists crossed. The stretch lasted three seconds. Then sit back down. Ethan noticed because the movement entered his peripheral vision at the same time every day. Eleven. Three seconds. Back to signing.

One day Tom set a can of sparkling water on the desk between them. Not in the middle. Slightly on Ethan’s side.

“Grabbed an extra.”

“Thanks.”

Ethan opened it. The hiss. The tab cracked. Cold aluminum against his palm.

Tom opened his own. Same hiss. They drank at the same time. Didn’t look at each other.

Approved. Signed. Next.

Approved. Signed. Next.

Every day. Alarm at seven. Subway. Crowded. Hand on the overhead strap. Metal. Cool. The cool of many people’s accumulated grip. His shoulder ached by the third stop. He switched hands. The other strap was warm — someone had just let go.

Workstation. Nine o’clock. Pending review: 220. The chair had molded to the previous person’s posture. His lower back didn’t fit the curve. He shifted. Shifted again. After a week he stopped shifting.

Pen in hand. The curve of his grip the same as his signature. Two thousand times a day, the same arc. Wrist still. Fingers moving. By afternoon the pen had warmed to the temperature of his hand and he couldn’t feel it anymore — the pen and the hand had become the same temperature, the same object.

Lunch. Cafeteria tray. Seven dollars. Chicken and rice. Vegetables yesterday’s color. Eating at his desk, the fork in his right hand, the mouse in his left, chewing and clicking in alternation.

Three o’clock, his hand ached. Set the pen down. Fingers curled and stretched. The middle knuckle cracked. On the desk, a groove where the pen rested. Not from him. From the person before. And the person before that. The groove was deeper than one person could make.

Break room. Filling water. Ran into someone. His badge: Strategic Decision Support. Fifth floor. Also filling. A stainless steel thermos with a sticker on it. A mountain, stylized. The kind you get at a tech conference. He filled it and left. They didn’t know each other. But they met here at three every day. Their cups touched the same spigot.

He was on his phone, speaking low: “This version can go straight to the executive meeting.”

He also looked at AI output. But he knew in three seconds whether it worked.

His salary was ten times Ethan’s. Same building. Same water cooler. Same spigot. Different floors.

Five thirty. Computer off. In the instant before the screen went black he could see the dust on it.

Outside, the light had shifted. The buildings across the street had turned orange on their western faces. A woman on the sidewalk below was walking a dog. The dog stopped at a fire hydrant. She waited. Her shadow long on the pavement. Ethan didn’t watch. He was putting on his jacket.

One afternoon. Quiet. The hum of air conditioning. The printer at the far end of the room cycling. Warming up, printing nothing, going to sleep. Tom’s chair creaked. Ethan’s pen touched paper.

A report came through. Consumer decision-making analysis. AI-written. Framing effect. Anchoring bias. The paragraphs clean. Smooth. From premise to evidence to conclusion with nothing wasted. Better than what he’d written in college. Better organized, better cited, better reasoned.

He read it twice. Not checking. Reading. Like reading someone else’s paper in a journal, except nobody wrote it, and reading it he felt the beginning of something — the way a well-constructed argument could pull you along its logic the way a river pulls a —

Signed it.

Beside him, Tom was also signing. The pen at his angle. The creak.

Lunch. The people from upstairs went to the sushi place next door. Sometimes walking past the floor-to-ceiling window. Wood tables inside, chopstick rests, small plates. Someone picking something up with chopsticks. The color of fish. Pink-orange. Through the window you couldn’t taste it but you could see the color. Fog on the window. The warm inside meeting the cold outside. His finger brushed the glass. Wiped a small clear patch. A sharper view inside. His finger moved on. The fog crept back.

On the wall behind his workstation, a sign. “Learn to coexist with AI. Empower a brighter future.” Plastic. The corner was peeling. Someone had written a small word in pencil beneath the peel – too small to read. Ethan leaned close once. Still couldn’t read it. Never tried again.

One night. The apartment. In the folding chair. Lights off. Light from outside coming in. Across the street, a few windows lit. Someone walking. A shadow. Left to right. Gone. Light off.

Calculated. Since starting, he’d signed roughly twenty-seven hundred reports.

What had he looked at.

Nothing.

On his phone, he scrolled to Marcus’s chat. The last message, months ago. “You doing okay?” Ethan had sent it. No reply.

Tapped in. No new messages. Scrolled up. He’d sent “Library, meet you there.” Marcus had sent “ok.” One word.

Backed out.
