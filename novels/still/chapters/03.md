# Chapter 3: The Penumbra

The dorm faucet was broken — not broken exactly, but at a certain angle it seeped, drip by drip.

Ethan tried turning it. Right all the way, no seep. Loosened a little, seep. Loosened more, no seep. A little more, seep again.

Some angles seeped and some didn’t, with no pattern in between. He thought he’d found the angle that didn’t seep, but when he released his hand — seep. The force of his hand and the force of its absence were different.

Drip.

The intervals were uneven. Sometimes fast, sometimes slow. Counting – one, two, three – didn’t come – four, five – came. Too late. Next time – one, two – came. Too early.

The drops hit the stainless steel basin. Each one in a slightly different spot – center sounded dull, near the edge bright.

The instant of falling – the water and the metal of the faucet separating – a thin column of water connecting them – stretching – thinner and thinner – broke. The upper part retreated to the faucet mouth. The lower part caught up to the drop that had already fallen. Merged.

Jake came to wash his hands. “This thing again?”

“Yeah.”

He turned it twice. Tight. Walked away.

No more seeping.

March. Second semester, sophomore year, half over.

The weather had changed — not cold, not warm — in the morning he wore a jacket, at noon it was too hot so he took it off, and by afternoon it was cool again and he put it back. Jacket off, draped over his arm, and walking it slipped, and he picked it up and switched arms, and it slipped again.

The magnolias on campus had bloomed, white, the blossoms large, the petals like very thin porcelain — translucent, with a ring of faint purple at the edges. The flowers on bare branches with no leaves, only flowers, as if the branches had grown lamps.

The classroom was emptying. Consumer Psychology — sixty-three last semester, and roll call now showed eighteen.

Less than a third. Where the rest went — dropped the course or stopped coming. Some empty seats had bags on them, held for someone absent. Others had nothing, the fold-down desks lowered as though nobody had ever sat there.

The professor’s voice hadn’t changed. Same speed. Same finger-circles in the air. But the sound was different. With the room emptied, the echo grew. Empty chairs didn’t absorb sound.

Marcus’s premium AI had expired.

He told Ethan in the dining hall at lunchtime, his tray pushed to the side, his finger on the table surface absent-mindedly tracing the grain back and forth.

“Expired. Twenty-something a month.”

“I could –”

“You paid for me last time.” He looked at Ethan. His eyes weren’t large but they were steady. “Twenty-some dollars a month. I know you were helping. But after you help, the thing is still twenty-some a month. Next month it’s still twenty-some.”

The tone wasn’t anger. Wasn’t politeness. It was fact. Twenty-some a month. No matter who paid.

“So you –”

“Use the free one. It’s fine. Slower.”

He stood. Carried his tray away. His back – white T-shirt – the line of the shoulders, loose – not relaxed loose – unfilled.

A few days later Marcus was in a corner of the library. He saw Ethan, reached into his bag and took out the notebook. Blue cover. Corners worn. Opened it to the middle.

“Can you look at this for me?”

The notebook pushed toward him. Green pen. Handwritten. The writing smaller than last semester – conserving space – margins narrowed. It was about Ming Dynasty salt merchants’ transport routes. Not paper format. His own thinking – arrows, question marks, stretches of speculation, some parts crossed out and rewritten.

Ethan looked at it.

Words on paper. Green. The curves and pressure of the handwriting he recognized. He looked at those words. Line by line.

He didn’t know what to say.

Not that it was bad. He spent about ten seconds searching for his own judgment – good or not, right or not, interesting or not – that judgment used to be there. You looked and you knew. Now he looked at those words and what came first was what AI would say – “Clear argument but lacks primary source support” – “Consider adding specific data on the salt certificate system” – those phrases scrolled across the green handwriting like subtitles. He wanted to turn the subtitles off. Couldn’t turn them off. He wasn’t sure whether he was looking at these words with his own standards or standards that AI had calibrated for him.

His finger on the paper’s edge. Didn’t turn the page.

Marcus sat across from him. Waiting.

Ten seconds. Fifteen seconds.

He should say something. “This part is interesting.” “You could expand this section.” Anything. Anything at all.

His mouth didn’t open.

Not that he didn’t want to speak. It was that he wasn’t sure whether what came out would be his own or a patchwork evaluation assembled from fragments. That uncertainty – stuck between his throat and his mouth – blocking.

Marcus looked at him.

Not an angry look. A kind of confirmation. Confirming what.

He closed the notebook. Green pen clipped back inside. Into the bag. Zipper zzzt.

“It’s fine.”

It’s fine. Two words. His voice the same quiet as always. No reproach. No disappointment. Just a door closing. The closing made no sound.

Ethan opened his mouth slightly.

Marcus had already stood. The chair leg scraped the floor. He slung the bag strap onto his shoulder. The strap slipped on the shoulder bone – he didn’t adjust. Walked away.

After that, when he saw Marcus – dining hall, library, on the road – Marcus didn’t take out the notebook. Before, occasionally he’d say “look at this” or “help me think about this part.” Not anymore.

The relationship hadn’t changed. They still ate together. Still nodded when they crossed paths. But he’d taken something back. Taken it back very quietly. Ethan didn’t know when it happened – in the fifteen seconds of his hesitation, or when Marcus closed the notebook, or earlier – when he’d started using AI’s standards to think without knowing he was using them.

He only knew Marcus didn’t let him see the notebook anymore.

AI raised its prices.

Popped up while he was brushing his teeth. His phone on the bathroom counter. Screen lit.

“Dear user: In order to provide you with an enhanced service experience, effective next month, Premium pricing will be adjusted to –”

Up twenty dollars.

Twenty dollars. Freshman year Derek made thirty dollars – “a boba tea” – grinning.

Toothpaste foam had gone cold. Spat. Rinsed. Faucet. When he turned it tight, he held for a second. No seep. Walked away.

April. Group project. Four people.

Library. Four laptops. Four AI chat windows.

One person wrote prompts. One polished. One made the slides. The last one presented in front of the class.

Prompts, twenty minutes. Polish, ten. Slides, fifteen. Under an hour. Most of the time – waiting for AI to generate. While waiting: phones, coffee, other conversations.

The one presenting was Ethan. But standing there he knew – this was temporary.

Professor Harris. Spent an entire lecture arguing that AI cannot understand Kant’s thing-in-itself. His finger drew many circles – more than usual – the circles getting larger – as if digging holes in the air – but the holes held nothing. His voice – earnest – occasionally urgent – as if racing – but unable to locate the opponent.

“The thing-in-itself is not a knowledge problem. It’s a limit. AI can process everything you put into it. But it doesn’t know there’s something you didn’t put in. It doesn’t know the limit. Because for it the limit doesn’t exist.”

Walking past the podium after class – his laptop was open. On the screen – an AI chat window. The last message: “Please organize the major contemporary philosophical debates around Kant’s concept of the thing-in-itself.”

Looked for one second. His laptop. His AI. He’d just argued that AI couldn’t understand this very thing. He’d used AI to prepare the lecture.

Walked on.

Mid-April. Night. Lights off.

Chris’s voice in the dark.

“Do you think any of what we’re learning actually matters?”

Nobody answered.

“Consumer psych. Behavioral econ. Cognitive biases. AI knows more about all this than we do. Right?”

Nobody answered. Ben’s breathing had steadied.

“So why are we learning it?”

In the distance – some other dorm – music – very far – couldn’t make out the melody – only the bass coming through the walls.

Jake: “Go to sleep.”

Quiet.

The curtain billowed once and fell back. Four people breathing. Not in sync.

May. A headline on his phone.

A new deep space probe. AI-designed. One-tenth the traditional timeline.

One-tenth.

His father said “thirty-some years” last year. Thirty-some years becomes three.

His phone went dark.

The employment advisory appeared in April, posted behind the glass of the bulletin board. A list of fields with “significant shifts in employment outlook.” Ethan’s major wasn’t on it. Jake’s was.

June. The insects’ call had changed.

Not three calls and a pause anymore. Sporadic. Sometimes two, then silence. Sometimes one and a stop. Sometimes five or six in a row with uneven spacing – no rhythm.

Ethan lay in bed and listened. Tried to count. Couldn’t. Not three calls and a pause. Not any-number-of-calls and a pause.

Were they the same ones? Or had last year’s died – and the new ones didn’t know to count to three?

He didn’t know. The insects called. He listened. But his mind didn’t follow the way it had last year. Last year he could go from the insect’s rhythm to “it doesn’t know anyone is listening” and from there to somewhere far.

This year. Insects calling. He listened. Listened for a while. His hand picked up his phone.

Summer break. Home.

Sitting on the couch – sunlight coming through the gap in the curtains – a shaft of light – dust floating in it – some fast, some slow, some spinning – spinning, a flash – like a very small star – spinning out of the shaft – going dark – invisible – but still there.

Last year he’d watched for an entire afternoon. Where does each speck of dust come from? He didn’t know. But last year he could stay inside not-knowing for a long time. Not-knowing was itself a place to dwell.

This year – watched a while – three minutes – his hand picked up the phone.

Nothing on it. Just that the feeling of watching dust – not knowing what you’re watching but watching – had broken. Used to be it didn’t break.

Three minutes, then his finger reached for the phone. Not that he told his hand to reach. The hand went on its own. Picked it up. Screen lit. Nothing there. Set it down. Looked at the dust again. Thirty seconds. Picked it up again.

The dust was still in the shaft of light. It didn’t need to be watched.
