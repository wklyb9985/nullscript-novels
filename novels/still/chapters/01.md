# Chapter 1: The Same Dining Hall

The acceptance letter arrived at the end of July in a kraft paper envelope, thick, and when Ethan pressed his fingers against it he felt a nap — not cloth nap, but the fibers of the paper itself, which hadn’t been fully pressed flat, standing just enough that they pushed back against his thumb the way the underside of a laurel leaf had once pushed back when he was a child, that fine fuzz running smooth in one direction and offering the gentlest resistance in the other, a force so small it seemed to say slow down — and the envelope’s nap was like that, yielding when he pressed and springing back when he released, the fibers remembering their own shape.

But if you pressed too hard they didn’t come back. He’d tried. Thumb down, released, and there was a small circle on the kraft paper, slightly shinier than the rest, where the fibers had collapsed for good.

His hand shook when he tore it open, not from nerves but because his heartbeat had gotten ahead of itself, the pulse traveling down through his arms to his fingertips, which tapped against the paper with a tiny percussion he could feel but couldn’t stop.

His father stood behind him with a hand on his shoulder, not resting but placed, the whole palm spread open so that the weight landed neither heavy nor light, and even through his shirt Ethan could feel the callus on the side of the index finger, yellowed and hard, the same callus that years ago, when his father had passed him a bowl across the table, had pressed against Ethan’s small fingers — and he’d tried to pick it off with his thumbnail, scraping at it, unable to budge it, until his father pulled his hand away. Later he understood: it had grown there from years of gripping tools, and once it grew it didn’t leave. Part of his father’s body had become the material of his work.

“Not bad,” his father said, and the voice came from just above the top of Ethan’s head.

His mother stood beside them without speaking, but the breath from her nose touched the skin behind his ear.

The letter inside had a university seal in dark red, and the paper was cheaper than the envelope — slick, no nap — a fingernail dragged across it like skating on very thin ice. He looked for his name twice, the first time too fast, and the second time he went line by line until he found it on the third line: Ethan Moss, printed there as if it had always been waiting.

Over the next few days the envelope sat on the kitchen table uncollected, leaning against the wall, and each time he passed — on his way to the kitchen, to the bathroom, coming back from outside — the kraft-paper color caught his peripheral vision, like touching it without reaching out.

The train was in late August.

He barely slept the night before, not from excitement but from something that didn’t know where to go, not heavy but present, like something extra in his pocket that he kept checking for. He got up in the middle of the night for water, the kitchen dark, his hand finding the faucet handle by feel — metal, cool — and when the water ran it was startlingly loud in the silence, filling the whole room with its sound. He took two sips and set the glass down and wiped his hand on his shorts, and back in bed the pillow had gone cold, the warmth having leaked out while he was away, and he had to warm it over again.

Morning came before full light. Crouching to tie his shoes, his fingers touched the tongue of the sneaker and found it damp — washed yesterday, not dried all the way through, the canvas still holding the night’s moisture.

The suitcase stood by the front door, blue, one wheel slightly canted where the axle had bent. His father had checked it, tried to fix it, couldn’t. “It’ll do,” he’d said, and when he said it he’d spun the wheel with his thumb, the bearing making a thin rattling sound that said it was loose.

His mother stood in the kitchen doorway in bare feet, her toes short, her nails trimmed even.

“Did you eat?”

“Yeah.”

He hadn’t. She didn’t press. She took two plastic bags from the counter — a bagel, an apple.

“For the road.”

In the side pocket of the suitcase, the apple’s shape pushed out through the fabric.

His father drove him to the station and carried the suitcase himself, the canted wheel pulling to the left so that he had to tilt his body to compensate, saying nothing about it. Nobody much on the roads at seven-something in the morning, just a bakery with steam rising from the oven vent, muffins golden and curved in the window, the counter girl flipping something with long tongs, a bit of grease popping. A tree by the road, not tall, with dew still on the leaves, and when Ethan’s arm brushed one it was cool, leaving a thin line of water on his skin that was gone in a few steps.

He walked behind his father, watching his back — the shirt was old, the collar washed soft and lighter than the fabric below, the shoulders moving one step at a time ahead of him, the suitcase tilting along beside.

On the platform there were people. A family of three stood next to them, the mother adjusting her daughter’s collar, the daughter squirming away, the father looking at his phone without looking at anything, his thumb still. And a boy in a high school hoodie, alone, earbuds trailing a wire from his collar, chewing something with crumbs on his lip.

His father’s back had sweat marks where the shirt stuck along both sides of the spine. Ethan didn’t know that his own back was wet.

“Study hard,” his father said. “Get out and find a good job.”

The callus touched Ethan’s wrist, and only then did he realize his father had reached out — the hand finding his arm without warning, hard and dry and rough at the edges, the man’s whole self concentrated into two or three square centimeters of skin.

The train moved. His father didn’t move on the platform.

Getting smaller — the gray shirt, the sweat marks, the back — all of it getting smaller. But the hand was the last part to disappear, raised and open, five fingers, holding there even after the rest of him had blurred.

Then the spot where he’d stood became a concrete pillar, and beyond the pillar was sky.

Ethan turned his head. Inside the car the air conditioning hummed, the seats were blue-gray, and his arm found the armrest — plastic, cool. In the seatback pocket ahead of him a magazine and a napkin, the napkin used, a brown mark on the corner where someone had wiped coffee or tea. The person was gone. The napkin remained.

The person in the next seat was sleeping, head tilted against the window, mouth open, and each exhale left a circle of fog on the glass that appeared and disappeared at a slow frequency, like breathing made visible. One hand still gripped a phone whose screen was dark, but the hand hadn’t let go.

Outside, plains. Late August green — deep, carrying gray, not the tender green of spring but a green that came from within, the leaves thickened and heavy. In the distance the heat made the line between green and sky tremble. A factory went by with two smokestacks that had no smoke, and beside it crops, green and gray-brown running side by side with a gray-white road between them and a pickup truck moving slow, something piled in the bed that he couldn’t see before it was gone.

A narrow creek whose surface flashed silver for an instant and was gone. A bridge with someone on it, bent at the waist, maybe fishing. His bent posture existed for about half a second at train speed, and then it didn’t.

In the distance a row of power poles, the wire sagging between each pair in an arc like a smiling mouth, one after another, passing at even intervals, like a pulse.

The apple his mother had given him sat uneaten in his lap. He took it out and held it, the skin cool in his palm — late August apples aren’t the sweetest — but holding it, the heat of his hand transferred into the fruit. He put it back.

The bagel too. He pressed it through the plastic and it gave, soft, with a layer of dry flour beneath the crust. He tore it open and bit and chewed twice, the sweetness faint, swallowed and bit again, and outside the world was moving while in his mouth he was chewing, two rhythms layered on top of each other.

The university was on the east side of the city, and out of the station the heat hit immediately — late August steam, the air holding water, his clothes sticking to his skin not from sweat but from the moisture in the air pressing directly onto him.

Many people at the exit, people holding signs in a row, and one man’s arm had a dark mole on the inner side that Ethan didn’t know why he noticed.

The suitcase rumbled on the brick pavers — click-click — and then over a stretch of asphalt the sound vanished, quiet as if the wheels had left the ground, and back to brick again — click-click-click. Someone in the plaza handing out flyers, a young guy, tanned, who held out a pink sheet — tutoring center — and Ethan took it, thin, the wind making it tremble in his hand, and he looked at it one second, folded it twice, stuffed it in his pocket, where later the washing machine got it and turned it into a wad of pulp that, pulled from the pocket, looked like the negative of gold dust.

The campus gates were crowded. Click-click-click, one step, two clicks of the wheel.

There was a tree. Massive, the canopy round, leaves so dense the branches were invisible, and on the ground beneath it circles of light — flat on the pavers, flat against the suitcase edge, flat on the top of his shoe. When the wind blew, all the circles trembled first, then slowly shifted, and two that were touching merged into brighter white, and when they separated a thin line of dark leaked between them.

The roots had pushed up out of the ground, thick, reaching from soil into the seam of the concrete — one alive, one dead, the alive one growing, the dead one waiting — and between them the concrete had cracked.

Under the tree a girl sat on her suitcase with a piece of paper in her hand, a slight furrow in her brow. The wind blew her hair and she blocked it with one hand, a few strands escaping between her fingers. She didn’t know he was watching.

He stood for a while — not watching her, just standing. The brick underfoot was hot, the heat rising through his shoe soles, and above him the tree shade was cool, and he was a person between two temperatures.

The dorm was on the third floor, and the hallway smelled of disinfectant, laundry detergent, and something else — concrete and dust and time mixed together — and smelling it he knew this wasn’t home.

On the stairwell landing a window stood open, hot air pouring in and mixing with the corridor’s cool, and someone had written on the wall in marker, illegible, covered by someone else’s writing, several layers stacked so that the bottom one had blurred. Each layer was a person who had stood here with a pen in hand, written something, and left. The hand left. The writing stayed.

The railing was iron with gray-green paint that had chipped off in places, exposing rust, reddish-brown, and his hand touched it going up — rough where the rust was, smooth where the paint held, the texture changing under his fingers as they slid from one surface to the other. His fingers knew the difference.

He opened the door to four beds, bunks, and the upper bunk by the window was his, sheets laid out in blue and white stripes. His hand pressed down on the mattress — cool — the coolness of cotton slow to tell you something: no one had been here in a long time.

He set his pillow down and the laundry detergent smell drifted out from the pillowcase his mother had washed — a floral scent, not real flowers, laundry detergent pretending to be flowers. But eighteen years of pretend-flower smell was the smell of home.

Bags set down. Suitcase shoved under the bed where it hit the bed leg with a sound. The charger was buried under the clothes, and pulling it out he brought a sock with it, which dropped onto the terrazzo floor, gray, nearly invisible against the gray-white surface. He picked it up. The zipper — zzzt — the last tooth catching, pulling back, passing through.

The room was only him.

He sat on the edge of the lower bunk, not his, the springs bouncing once under his weight. The terrazzo floor had black chips in it, cross-sections of stone polished flat.

The curtain was off-white with the bottom edge yellowed, and the light came through so that he could see the weave, each crossing where the threads thickened dimming the light at those points. When the wind was strong the curtain billowed inward, as if someone were pushing from outside, and sucked back, and billowed again.

He stood at the window. A small crack in the glass started from the corner and ran about an inch, like a strand of hair, and through the crack the world outside bent slightly — shifted.

Below was a road lined with sycamores, late August green, and a person walking it slowly, hands clasped behind the back, not a student. Walking through a tree’s shade the shadow covered him, and walking out the sunlight found him again — covered, out, covered, out — until he reached the end and turned the corner and was gone.

Ethan still stood there, not knowing how long. Time was different like this, in a new place, alone, looking at a road you didn’t know — time slowed, or not slowed exactly, but he’d started noticing it. The sun was moving. The shadows were moving. Wind came and wind left. Warmer, cooler. He was here, nowhere else to be.

Another person appeared, pushing a bicycle with a white plastic bag in the basket, walking very slowly, and passed. The road was empty again.

Empty, the road was longer than when someone was on it.

His roommates arrived that afternoon.

Chris came first — half a head shorter than Ethan, round face, two dimples visible even when he wasn’t smiling. He walked in ahead of his mom, who was carrying the suitcase, her temples showing gray, her face round in the same way as her son’s, the genetics of roundness. He said “Hey” and started stuffing things into the closet, grabbing a wad and pressing it in and when it didn’t fit, changing the angle and pressing again.

His mom stood beside him watching. “That goes on top.” “Get the towel out.” He mm-hmmed twice, and a shirt popped out, and he pressed it back.

“Where you from?”

“Ohio. You?”

“Illinois. Downstate.”

“Pretty close.” He kept stuffing.

His mom left, standing at the door for a moment — “Air out the comforter” — and he said he knew, and she left. He looked at the closed door, sat on his bed with both hands bracing the edge, and swung his legs twice.

“You know where the dining hall is?”

“Out the door, take a left I think.”

“Let’s go.”

The two of them went out into the long hallway and turned left and took the stairs, passing a trash can on the first floor with an apple core beside it, not yet brown, the teeth marks showing two neat arcs. Out the building the sun was blazing, the shadows short and round, and they stepped on them as they walked.

“Hot,” Chris said.

“Yeah.”

They walked a stretch without talking, their shoes making two different sounds — Chris’s flip-flops slapping, Ethan’s sneakers muffled — alternating as they went. They passed the bulletin board and the glass reflected light, their two shadows warping across it and vanishing as they walked by.

Jake arrived next — thin, a white fitness band on his wrist. He came in without saying much, set down his bags, and sat on the bed edge looking at his phone, his thumb not moving, the screen showing a chat window where nothing had been typed. His mom stood at the door for a while and said something in a low voice, and he said mm, and she left. He set the phone face-down on the bed and went to the bathroom, opened the faucet and closed it, came out with wet hands, and wiped them twice on his jeans.

Ben arrived last, in the evening, with a small suitcase that was heavy. He stepped in and looked around first — not at the people, at the layout — left, right, up, down.

“Top bunk, right?”

He climbed up, the ladder creaking twice, and went quiet.

The first night. Four people, four beds, and the sound of someone turning over made a spring complain. Outside the window a streetlight cast amber light that hit the ceiling and made a distorted quadrilateral.

No air conditioning, just heat. Blanket pulled only to the waist. The wind came through the window and brought a moment of cool that stopped and was replaced by heat again.

But the wind carried grass smell with it. Night grass is different from daytime grass — in the day you have to lean close to smell it, but at night it comes on its own, the dew washing the scent from the blades so that earth and leaf and water arrive together as a wet green.

Then insects.

He didn’t know what kind. Not crickets — crickets chirp high and sharp — this was a low hum with grainy pulses layered on top, fixed in one place, coming from the grass or from a tree.

It had a rhythm. Three calls, pause. Three calls, pause. As if counting something, or not counting, the body simply vibrating that way: pulse-pulse-pulse and rest. Pulse-pulse-pulse and rest.

Ethan counted along. Three calls, pause. Occasionally four, and then a longer pause, and back to three.

Chris turned over and the spring sounded inside the insect sound — one short, one continuous — the spring brief and gone, the insect still going.

The insect kept on. Three calls, pause.

It didn’t know anyone was listening. It was doing its own thing. Ethan happened to be here. Its rhythm wasn’t for him. But he was listening, and he was counting, and he was making its rhythm his own. It didn’t know.

Three calls, pause.

The dining hall was north of the dorm, about a five-minute walk — out the door, take a left, past the bulletin board with its glass-covered posters and notices, turn right at the end, cross the small plaza with four stone tables carved with chess grids nobody played on — and then the dining hall.

Walking there in the early morning, a little after seven, with the sun just above the tree line and the light slanting so that the shadows stretched long from the tree roots across the road, he passed a row of bicycles in all colors. One had a flat front tire and a flyer in the basket that had been rained on and dried, the paper wrinkled like a face that had been clenched and smoothed out. Another had bird droppings on the seat, white, dried, set into the vinyl.

People walked at the shadow’s edge, stepping on it where it moved, their own shadow and the tree’s layered together, inseparable.

The windows of the ground floor lined up in a row with steam drifting from each one, white fluorescent lights inside, one tube flickering — not flickering exactly, but brightening and dimming at a frequency so fast it was unnoticeable unless you kept looking, existing at a frequency you’d assumed didn’t exist.

The serving lady had a red string bracelet on her wrist, and when she scooped the food the ladle went down, turned, came up, her wrist rotating, the food landing on the plate with the same arc every time — she’d been doing it so long that her wrist remembered.

A chicken thigh landed on his plate and left a small crater. Grease seeped from the meat along the boundary where it touched the rest of the food, the line ragged, not straight, traveling between the gaps until it couldn’t travel anymore and stopping.

He carried the tray to find a seat, the stainless steel scratched dense and crossing at different depths, each scratch made by a fork or knife before him — how many people had eaten on this tray, one scratch, one person, all on the same surface. His finger followed the deepest one, left to right, curved, with a section in the middle especially bright where it had been worn smooth by the most fingers. Next to it a shallow one, like a pencil line, visible only when the light shifted — made at a different time, with different force, in a different direction, but on the same plane.

He found a table and sat down, the plastic seat warm from the previous person, a trace of body heat still there.

People eating around him — forks on trays, chewing, spoons scraping metal — all the sounds layered into a kind of hum, not any particular sound but all of them added together becoming an environment, a warm hum with people inside it.

Everyone eating in the same dining hall — people in flip-flops and people in dress shoes, people with Southern accents and people who ate quietly and left — the trays held at about the same height, the feet covering about the same distance from the counter to the table, the sound of setting the tray down about the same.

Someone twisted open a bottle of hot sauce and the vinegar smell shot out from the bottle mouth, sharp, hanging in his nose for a second before fading. Dark liquid ran a short way across a flour tortilla.

Chris sat down across from him with a tray fuller than Ethan’s — three items — and ate fast. “What’s your major” — chewing twice — “business econ” — chewing again — and his dimples deepened when he chewed on the left side, the right one especially.

“Same.”

“Oh.” He ate another bite. “So what are you gonna do after.”

“Don’t know.”

“Me neither,” he said, and grinned, both dimples deepening. He didn’t mind not knowing. Eighteen doesn’t mind.

When they finished they brought their trays to the return window, where a pair of hands behind the glass — plastic gloves, water on the gloves — took the tray, and when Ethan’s fingers touched those hands for an instant it was cool through a layer of plastic.

Stepping out of the dining hall, the sunlight poured over him — from the air-conditioned cold into the heat, like walking through a door with two kinds of weather on either side.

On the way back he passed the track, where someone was running alone, steady pace, footfalls hitting the red rubber — thud thud thud thud — the shadow of every granule on the surface too many to count.

The runner went one lap and then another, not looking at anyone, locked in his own rhythm, his breath inaudible at a distance but growing louder as he came around the near curve — hah, hah — and fading as he pulled away — and coming around again — hah, hah —

At the track’s edge, a stone bench, gray, with lichen on its surface — green, dried, dead, flattened, fused with the stone.

September’s days didn’t count. One day and the next weren’t very different.

Morning, the alarm, and his hand went out from under the blanket into the cool air, found the phone, silenced it. Awake. Brushed his teeth with mint foam in his mouth, water spots on the mirror from Chris splashing when he washed his face, dried into small circles, Ethan’s face appearing between those circles.

Class. End of class. Dining hall. Class. End of class. Dorm.

Said like that it sounds like repetition. But at the time it didn’t feel that way. Inside each day there was a lot of time spent doing nothing — waiting, walking, standing — and the nothing had texture.

Waiting for someone in the hallway, where the walls were pale green, institutional green, diluted too many times to really be green anymore. A mark on one wall where something had scraped it at about waist height, long, gray — probably a suitcase. Who scraped it didn’t matter. The scraping was done, the person gone. The mark was here.

He stood for about three minutes, and Chris came out. “Let’s go.” They went.

In the dining hall Chris said the fries today were soggy. Ethan said they were crispy two days ago, soggy yesterday.

“I told the lady. She said that’s how they come.”

He was especially earnest about this, his brow furrowed, even the dimples vanishing. Then he took a bite, chewed twice.

“Soggy today.”

Walking from the classroom building to the dorm, ten minutes, short enough not to think about the route, long enough to think about other things, his feet going from new concrete to old concrete — no difference in feel, but the color boundary was there.

Sycamore shadows — at noon short, tucked at the tree’s feet, and in the afternoon long, spread across the road, so that walking in the shade was slightly cooler and stepping out was hot again.

When there was nowhere to be — three o’clock, no class — he went back to the dorm, climbed to his upper bunk, and lay down.

The ceiling, white. A water stain that looked like something — he stared a long time — and didn’t look like anything. But he tried every time. Like a hand. No. Like a cloud. No. Like a country, though which country he didn’t know.

There was also a dead mosquito on the ceiling, stuck there with its wings spread, very small, hard to see unless you looked. But Ethan saw it. He saw it every day.

Lying there, the light on the ceiling slowly shifted as the sun moved, and when it reached the mosquito a shadow appeared — a small cross — and the light kept moving toward the wall corner, dimmer now. Afternoon.

Sometimes he thought about things, not specific things, just thoughts drifting by, touching down, leaving, another arriving, like things floating on the surface of water. He didn’t fish them out. They drifted past on their own.

He thought about something — the brick pavers — right, that afternoon walking, he’d stepped on a loose one and it rocked, and a little water squeezed out from underneath, gray, and under that brick there was a world that had been accumulating for who knows how long, stepped on and it seeped out, stepped off and it sank back, the water retreating —

He thought about something else — the chess grids on the stone tables in the plaza, the stone older than the carvings — who carved them, and why, when nobody played chess —

He stopped thinking. The light had reached the wall corner.

At night Jake made ramen, pouring boiling water in so that the steam hit his face, and covered the cup with a textbook. That book would have a water ring on its cover from that day forward.

When it was ready he poured milk into it.

“What are you doing?” Chris leaned over from the top bunk.

“It’s good. Try it.”

Ethan tried a bite. Salt and cream together, strange, but not bad — a flavor that shouldn’t exist but did.

Jake drank all the broth, set the bowl on the desk, and left a ring of heat on the surface.

Consumer Psychology was the first class that interested Ethan — not the content, not the textbook stuff about framing effects and anchoring bias, but the person teaching it. Professor Harris, whose hair was combed back and who drew circles in the air with his index finger whenever he reached an important concept, as though the concept were inside the circle. But the circle was empty.

The classroom was in the old building, with a narrow hallway whose wood floors creaked when you walked — a deep sound, the whole plank bending and springing back. Some planks were darker than those beside them where more feet had walked, the finish worn off to expose the lighter color beneath.

The doorknob was brass, not bright, dulled by too many hands. When his hand gripped it, his fingers curving into the brass curve, it was cool at first but quickly warm.

The third week he turned in a paper — electronic submission — and sat in front of the computer all afternoon, the Word document white, the cursor blinking.

Near the library someone was playing guitar, not inside but on the quad, the sound coming in through the window. He didn’t know what they were playing, and the chords weren’t quite right, but that sound while writing — chords arriving from a distance, not quite right — didn’t intrude. It was there, in the same afternoon.

He wrote slowly, not because he didn’t know what to write but because he was thinking. Framing effects — subjects who chose thought they were choosing, but the wording had already made the choice for them. That wasn’t just psychology. That was — and his fingers stopped on the keyboard, four of them resting on the F and J row, the fingertip temperature and the keycap temperature slowly converging, cold when first placed, indistinguishable after a while.

Midway through he went to the bathroom and stood in the hallway, where through the window the sycamores stood in the afternoon light, past four, side-lit so that each trunk was half bright and half dark, the line between them running straight from top to bottom.

He washed his hands under the motion-sensor faucet — hand approached, water came; moved away, water stopped. The water knew he was there. It didn’t know who he was.

The hallway window was open, and the wind touched the sweat on his forehead and cooled it. He stood a moment. Went back to writing.

Three and a half hours to finish. Outside the window it was dark. Walking back to the dorm past the sycamores, the streetlights turned the leaves from green to a green with yellow in it, the artificial light pushing the daytime colors toward warm.

A cat, gray and white, crouching under a streetlight. It looked at him with yellow eyes — two small lamps — and didn’t run. He didn’t stop either. As he passed, its head turned slightly, and then he was past it.

Back at the dorm Chris was gaming with headphones on, his mouth saying something — “left side, left side” — his body in the room but somewhere else.

Ben lay on the top bunk with his phone propped by the pillow, the blue light from above making his face glow. He turned a page. The sound of paper.

Jake wasn’t there. On his desk a bag of bread sat half-eaten, the plastic bag’s mouth open, the bread’s cross-section facing up, showing air pockets of different sizes where the yeast had breathed — the breath trapped by the dough, becoming these holes — and the yeast had died, but its breathing stayed in the bread.

Ethan climbed up to his bunk and lay down. The paper’s sentences were still turning in his head — not the content, but the abandoned approaches, the directions he hadn’t written, the thoughts that had branched from one fork to another and never appeared in the paper but had appeared, between his fingers and the keys, in the three-plus hours before the temperature transferred.

The paper came back with four words: “Insightful. Keep it up. B+.”

He turned it over. Blank back. Turned it back. “Insightful” in the upper right corner, red ink, Professor Harris’s handwriting — small, leaning right, each stroke quick but unconnected, each word standing alone, like the way he spoke, short sentences, period, short sentences.

B+, not an A. But “insightful.” From two thousand words, Harris had seen those three-plus hours.

The paper got tucked into a notebook between pages seven and eight. Later, after several moves, he didn’t know where it was. But “insightful” was still there — not on the paper anymore, but somewhere.

The bulletin board by the plaza was covered in student org recruitment posters — drama club, guitar club, debate team — hand-drawn in marker on white paper, the letters tilted and cramped with the feeling of something alive. One corner had been peeled up by wind where the thumbtack hadn’t held, and it flapped like a hand waving.

At the bottom of the debate team poster, one line: “Learn to coexist with AI — the future of human-machine collaboration and you.” He didn’t know who’d written it. At the time every word felt large. Learn. To. Coexist.

Derek was someone he met through debate, the kind of person you noticed first in any room — not because he was good-looking but because he moved, constantly, his hands picking up a water bottle and setting it down and switching hands, his mouth throwing a few words to this person and turning to say a few to that one, his eyes like searchlights scanning and stopping and lingering on anything interesting.

He couldn’t sit still. During discussions he paced from the whiteboard to the window and the window to the door, talking while walking, as though his thinking needed his feet to push it — feet stop, brain stops.

He’d designed a poster for the student council using AI. Thirty dollars.

“Thirty bucks,” he told Ethan, grinning. “A boba tea.”

Thirty dollars, a boba tea, one poster — in freshman year these were small things. Thirty dollars was endearing. The gap between money and work was tiny: one poster, thirty dollars, one boba tea. Nothing in between.

That day the boba was Derek’s treat, Ethan included, walking down the road with straws through the lids, the tapioca pearls sucked up from the bottom hitting his mouth springy, bouncing back when he bit down, needing a second bite to break them.

An October afternoon with boba in hand, not going anywhere in particular, just walking.

Sunlight on the road and tree shadows on the road, and walking through a shadow was cool for a moment, and out of it warm, cool and warm alternating, as if the road itself were breathing.

They ran into Jake, alone, a bag of bread in his hand.

“Where you going?”

“Nowhere.”

“Come on.”

Three of them walked a stretch without talking, three shadows of different lengths — Derek’s the longest because he was tallest, Jake’s the thinnest, Ethan’s in the middle — sometimes overlapping when they walked close, sometimes apart.

At a fork five minutes later Derek went left — “Debate practice tomorrow” — and Jake went right — “I’m heading back.”

They dispersed.

Ethan stood alone at the fork. Left, right, ahead.

Ahead. No destination, but a road, and sunlight — October sunlight, not hot anymore, just warm, resting on his shoulders without weight.

He walked.

There was a small shop at the base of the dorm building with a faded awning over the door and a cooler humming by the entrance, its glass front smudged at handle height. The door frame’s paint was peeling, the bare wood exposed where moisture had swollen it soft — press a finger and the fibers gave.

Some evenings he went down to buy small things — a bottle of water, tissues, occasionally an ice cream bar.

The owner was a middle-aged woman with an apron pocket stuffed with loose change that clinked when she walked and a calculator on the counter whose 5 and 8 were worn smooth because she didn’t need to look.

“Dollar fifty.”

He paid and took it.

He opened it outside and drank, the water cool, the cap’s seal breaking with a snap, the broken ring left at the bottle’s neck forming an incomplete circle.

Under the streetlight, a moth circled the lamp globe, its wing shadow thrown on the ground many times bigger than the moth itself. The lamp didn’t move.

October night. Water cool. Person warm.

The first time he used AI was in October.

The university had given everyone a free account — campus edition, fifty conversations a day — and his RA posted it in the group chat, a paragraph and a link. He clicked, registered, and was done.

He opened it in the dorm one evening while Chris gamed beside him.

The interface was white, with a text box and a cursor blinking, waiting for him to speak.

“Hello.”

“Hello! I’m your AI assistant. I’m eager to help you with anything you need. How can I assist you today?”

Eager. He stared at the word for two seconds — eager, a word running too hot, like bath water turned up past comfortable.

He changed “eager” to “happy.”

“Hello! I’m happy to help you with whatever you need. What can I do for you?”

Right. Happy was happy.

He spent ten minutes editing the chat, adjusting phrasing and tone. “I’d be delighted to” became “sure.” “I’m more than happy to assist you with” became “I can.” The AI listened every time, changing once and changing again.

When he was done he read it through. Good — a normal way of talking.

Then he tried something.

“Can you find me the original framing effect experiment? The Tversky and Kahneman paper.”

Three seconds. The answer appeared — complete, accurate, the citation format correct. He flipped through last semester’s notes to page seven, where the same experiment sat in his own hand-copied abstract, the handwriting crooked, his hand having ached by the end from two hours in the library finding the paper.

Two hours. Three seconds.

He closed it.

November brought the cold.

The sycamore leaves started turning, the edges first, the serrations yellowing and working inward, and some leaves were half green and half gold, one side still summer, the other already winter. The brown ones fell when the wind blew, spinning in the air on paths that were never straight down, every leaf’s path different. One landed on Ethan’s backpack, fan-shaped, the veins spreading like fingers, and he picked it up and looked at it and put it in his pocket, where later it crumbled into a few pieces of brittle gold in the corner, like gold dust.

After the cold came, the dining hall steam was more visible — white, pouring from the exhaust vents, dissipating gradually, the transition from visible to invisible a continuous thinning.

After the cold came, there was a soup station. Chicken noodle, the bowl steaming, and when he held it the steam from the rim touched his chin, damp and warm, and from mouth to throat to stomach the entire path was warmed.

After the cold came, the radiator in the dorm made its sounds — occasional, a tink of metal expanding, only audible when it was quiet, loudest at night. Tink. Wait a long time. Tink again. As if the pipes could only say one word. The radiator was cast iron, vertical pipes in a row like a fence, and touching it you felt warmth — not hot, just warm enough to keep touching. After finishing homework he’d put his hand on it, five fingers flat on the iron surface, the heat arriving at all five fingertips at the same time.

After the cold, more layers of clothing. Standing outside the door for a moment while the zipper went zzzt, the metal teeth biting together one by one. Hands in his pockets, the pocket lining flannel, warm, his hands curling inside, not doing anything, just resting.

One afternoon with no class, Ethan walked alone to a street behind campus.

Nothing special — food places, a fruit stand with oranges stacked in a pyramid where the top one looked about to fall, apples each with a small sticker that when peeled off left a square mark.

There was a shoe repair place, and an old man in front of a sewing machine — the cobbler kind — stitching a shoe, his foot on the treadle, up and down like a heartbeat.

Ethan stood beside him and watched for a while. The man didn’t look up.

His hands were dark, glue and leather stained into the creases so deep they wouldn’t wash out, nails short, the space under them dark and permanent. Knuckles thick, the skin stretched tight over bone, no extra flesh.

His father’s hands were like that. The yellow on the side of the index finger.

He watched a few minutes and walked on.

He bought a warm donut from the bakery on the corner, still hot, the glaze not yet set, sticky between his thumb and the paper bag. Biting into it the glaze cracked, the inside soft and warm and faintly yeasty, the sweetness concentrated where the glaze had pooled in the folds. Its heat in his hand traveled all the way up his wrist and stayed.

The debate tournament was in mid-December.

Two rows of chairs at the back of the classroom, and forty or fifty people had come — someone eating with the crinkle of a plastic bag, someone taking a photo with a flash.

At the podium there was a microphone with a foam cover, gray, the pores visible up close, dusty, and when he spoke into it he smelled old cloth and rust mixed together.

The backs of his knees were sweating, his pants sticking to the crease. Lights above. The audience below, their faces indistinguishable.

His turn. Notes on the podium.

Then the notes weren’t enough.

Midway through, the words coming out of his mouth weren’t on the page anymore — a thought about framing effects and language, and he followed it, followed it a long way, until the thought finished itself, and then he stopped.

He stopped and heard applause. Not a lot. But some.

Off the stage his legs went soft for two seconds, the damp fabric behind his knees separating from the skin, cool air slipping in.

Derek came over and slapped his back, hot palm. “Dude. That was sick.” Something in his mouth — chewing, a piece of gum — the word and the gum coming out together.

Before finals the library was full, every seat taken, and walking in he felt that density — a full room where the air temperature ran two degrees warmer than the hallway.

Inside the door, shoes scuffing on the mat, gray and damp, trodden by countless soles. His own stuck slightly — two steps — and then dry again.

A corner seat. On the table a pencil left by the last person — yellow, HB, sharpened but not sharp, six-sided with the faintest wood grain beneath the paint, the paint not quite covering everything.

The library lights were white, and everyone’s hair under the white light had a bright edge — rows of heads beneath the fixtures.

The girl beside him was working on problem sets, fast, pen moving nonstop with two-second pauses here and there. She gripped the pen very low, about an inch from the tip, and a red mark had formed on her finger where it pressed.

When the library was full — wrapped inside people — you were part of the density. Many people in the same space doing the same thing, not speaking, but present. Body heat present. Carbon dioxide present. The air warmed two degrees.

Across the table sat a guy he didn’t know, thin, writing in a notebook with a green pen, his wrist moving with each stroke. Under the desk, canvas sneakers, the heels worn down at an angle, the rubber thinned to nothing on the outer edge, one more month and the sole would wear through. He didn’t look up. Ethan didn’t say anything. Two people at the same table, not speaking.

A guy had fallen asleep at his desk with his arm pillowed on a book, a line of drool at the corner of his mouth, and the open page was bending to the curve of his arm — when he woke and moved, the paper would spring back, but not all the way. A bend would remain.

Walking out after the last exam in late December, the outside air hit him — cold, dry — and a deep breath made his nostrils sting, not with pain but with clarity. Cold air is a form of clarity: it told you the nasal cavity is this shape, the windpipe this long, the lungs this big. The interior of the body measured by cold air.

The hallway lights were motion-activated, switching on as he walked in — footsteps, light — and off behind him, then on again as he moved forward, the hallway waking in segments.

Back in the room Chris was gaming, the headphone cord swaying against his chest. Ben on his bunk with his phone, blue light from above, face lit blue. Jake wasn’t there. His water bottle on the nightstand, empty.

Upper bunk, and the pillow was cold again — cold every night — and every night he warmed it over.

Winter break. Home.

The smell of home hit him in the doorway — not cooking, not any one thing, but everything together: walls, floors, closets, the dust on the curtains. Living in it you couldn’t smell it. Coming back, you could.

By the shoe rack a new pair, his father’s, brown, the leather creased, worn but only a few times, the soles still clean.

His mother was in the kitchen under the range hood’s hum. “You’re back?” “Mm.” “Hungry? Almost ready.”

Three at the dinner table. Pot roast that had been cooking since morning, the meat falling apart when his father pressed the fork in, the carrots so soft they’d lost their edges, everything sitting in a brown liquid that smelled of bay leaves and onion. Green beans with the ends snapped off, glistening. A bowl of mashed potatoes, still steaming, a knob of butter melting in the center.

His father reached across the table and put a thick piece of the roast on Ethan’s plate, the fork holding it steady, not saying anything.

The pot roast was his mother’s recipe from as far back as he could remember — the same dutch oven, the same low heat, the same way the meat shredded along its grain when you pulled it with a fork, the fibers separating into long strands, and if you pressed a piece between your tongue and the roof of your mouth the fat dissolved first and then the meat, a warmth that wasn’t temperature but flavor, specific to this kitchen and no other.

They finished. Plates clean — every last bit — his father’s too. This habit, a clean plate, came from somewhere he didn’t know, some meal before he was born.

After dinner on the couch, TV on. A documentary about a deep space probe.

“Look at this,” his father said. A silver-gray shape against a black background. “How long has it been flying?”

Ethan checked. “Over thirty years.”

“Thirty-some years.” He repeated it and said nothing more.

Thirty-some years — a thing flying for thirty-some years. While it flew, on Earth, how many people were born and died. It was still flying, not knowing what was happening on Earth, following a trajectory laid down thirty-some years ago.

His father’s profile in the TV light, blue-white-blue-white, the wrinkles deeper in that flickering. He was older than Ethan remembered — not a lot, a little, but that little was certain.

“How much did that cost?” he asked, pointing at the probe.

“Billions? Let me check —”

“Forget it. Billions.”

The way he said “billions” was different from the way he said “thirty years.”

The last night.

Three people in the living room, TV on.

His mother on the couch knitting a coaster in blue yarn, the needle going in and out of the loops, her fingers quick but small. She didn’t watch the needle — her hands knew. The yarn pulled from the ball on her lap, which rolled slightly, caught by her knee, and she kept going.

His father sat beside her leaning against the couch back, feet up on the coffee table, one slipper about to fall off.

Ethan sat at the other end with his phone in his hand but not looking at it, the screen dark, his finger on the glass — smooth, cool — not scrolling, just resting.

On the coffee table, peanuts in a bowl, shells in a small dish. His father took one now and then. His mother took one. Ethan took one. Three hands reaching into the same dish.

His father cracked the shells the way he always had — right hand picking one up, front teeth biting, left hand catching the shell — for as long as Ethan could remember. The shells piled in the dish, which was white with a small chip at the rim from some time he didn’t know, the broken edge rough where the ceramic showed beneath the glaze.

This scene — three people, this room, this light, the TV blue-white and the lamp yellow blending together —

It didn’t mean anything. Just three people in a room. Tomorrow one of them would leave. But not yet.

His father’s breathing deepened, nearly asleep, sitting, his head tilted slightly.

His mother glanced at him. Didn’t wake him. Kept knitting.

Outside the window, insects — different from the campus ones — a continuous hum with no rhythm. But listening longer, inside the hum there were tiny rises and falls, like something you’d assumed was flat that wasn’t flat.
