# Chapter 5: The Empty Mold

Spring. March. Second semester of junior year.

The magnolias had bloomed on the quad. White. Large blossoms. The petals like very thin porcelain – translucent – a ring of faint purple at the edges.

He walked past them to the dining hall. Walked past them every day. After the third day he stopped seeing them. The bees hadn’t stopped. They moved from blossom to blossom, the petals dipping under their weight. The ground beneath the trees was covered in fallen petals, white, browning at the edges, and people walked over them without looking down.

Submitted resumes. AI-written. Input the job description – output the resume – changed the name and school – submitted.

Over sixty. Most didn’t reply. Sometimes three classmates applied for the same position – three resumes written by the same AI – names swapped – everything else the same. Three people’s differences flattened.

The phone buzzed. Career center push notification. A poster – suited silhouette, upward arrow.

Buzzed again. Another.

Dining hall. Tuesday.

Marcus was at the usual table. Ethan saw him from the tray line – white T-shirt – but the shirt was different now. The fabric had slipped from the shoulder – the shoulder bone couldn’t hold it – the fabric slid down and folded once. His collarbone visible from the neckline – two lines – from the base of the neck to the shoulder tip.

He was thinner. The face narrower. The cheekbones higher than Ethan remembered, or the flesh around them lower. Under the table, the canvas sneakers – the same ones – but the worn heel had gotten worse – the outer edge of the left sole had a hole now, a dark circle where rubber used to be.

“Hey.”

“Hey.”

His tray – vegetables, half a serving of rice. Stainless steel. Scratches. Same tray as freshman year.

Sat down. Dining hall sounds around them. Same sounds as freshman year. Forks, steam, talking. But the temperature he heard was different now.

Marcus ate slowly. Fork pausing before each bite. The pauses were longer than they used to be. He finished what was on his tray. Fork placed on the edge – parallel – neat.

“Find anything?”

“What?”

“Work.”

“No.”

The notebook was beside him on the bench. Blue cover. Corners worn worse now – the blue nearly gone – all white base. Green pen clipped inside.

Ethan wanted to say something. About the resume. He could help. He could write the prompts, get AI to generate something, send it over. He opened his mouth.

Marcus looked at him. The same steady look. He knew what Ethan was about to say.

“I’m going.” He picked up his tray. The remaining grain of rice slid to the tray’s low edge.

Ethan watched his back. White T-shirt. The line of the shoulders – not relaxed loose – unfilled. Under the shirt, the vertebrae. He could count them.

Library. Late afternoon. The same corner seat.

A notebook. Paper. Picked up a pen.

Pen tip on paper.

Stopped.

Not that his mind was empty. Things were in there. But they wouldn’t become words anymore. Crouching in there and not moving.

The pen tip left a dot. Ink seeped out. Very small. From where the pen touched, spreading in every direction – but uneven distances – the fibers ran different directions – some spreading sideways – some turning – the outermost edge wasn’t round. The color changing from center to edge – black – dark blue – blue – gray – white. The white part – untouched by ink – fibers still in their original alignment – undisturbed.

Stared at it for a few seconds. Two page-turns from someone nearby. Still only that dot on the paper.

Nothing on one side. Nothing on the other.

Set the pen down.

Scrolling his phone. A headline. One billion users. Twelve billion in losses. Stock price up. Thumb scrolled past. Another one. A forum post: “Switched AI providers. About the same. Saved eighty percent.” Thumb paused a moment. Scrolled past.

May. He went to the library to return a book and walked past the corner tables. Third floor. The area where Marcus always sat – left side, against the wall, near the window with the cracked pane.

Someone else’s things were on the table. A laptop. A phone charger coiled beside it. A coffee cup – paper – lipstick on the rim. Pink. The chair was pushed in at a different angle than Marcus pushed it.

Ethan stood there.

He looked at the table surface. The same scratches. The same gouge near the upper left corner where something had been carved. But no blue notebook. No green pen. The light from the window fell across the stranger’s laptop and made a white rectangle on the screen.

Sat down at the next table. Opened his laptop. Didn’t open anything. The screen was bright and his face was in it – reflected – dim against the white.

Fifteen minutes. Closed it. Left.

June. Before graduation.

Ethan searched for Marcus in the directory. Page by page. History department. Names starting with M. His finger sliding across the paper – not him – not him – not him –

Not there.

Went back and looked again. Line by line, letter by letter.

Not there.

Asked someone from history. “Marcus? Marcus Ward?” Thought for a moment. “I think he – transferred? Not sure.”

Another: “Dropped out, I think. I heard from somebody.”

A third person didn’t know who he was talking about.

Nobody knew where he’d gone.

Later, returning books at the library counter. On the counter, a book returned by someone – the library card slip had his name. The spine had a dog-ear. Opened to that page – no notes – no underlining – just a faint fingernail impression – on the third line. Couldn’t be sure it was his.

Closed it. Set it on the counter. Walked away.

Graduation ceremony. The quad. Cap and gown. Black. Synthetic. Hot. The cap square. The tassel from right to left – gold – swaying by his face in the wind. The sycamores were in full leaf, the canopy thick, and a breeze moved through the crowd carrying the smell of cut grass and sunscreen and someone’s cologne. Sprinklers had left dark half-circles on the lawn where the chairs didn’t cover.

On stage the president was speaking. Very far. Couldn’t make out the face.

“You are the builders of a new era –”

People beside him taking photos. Flash. Someone laughing. Someone looking for someone. A small child in the row ahead had climbed onto a chair and was reaching for a balloon tied to the armrest, the string taut, the balloon bobbing.

Marcus wasn’t there.

Caps thrown up. A row of black squares in the sky – spinning – falling. Some people picked theirs up. Some didn’t.

Packing up the dorm. The last time.

In the drawer – notebooks – scratch paper – a paper clip – a photo – four people in the dining hall – Chris’s dimples – Jake’s wristband – Ben in the back – half his face.

Photo went into the suitcase. Paper clip left on the desk.

Door closed. Locked. Key turned in. The RA took it, dropped it in a plastic bin – clink – his key mixed with everyone else’s.

Walked out of the dorm building. Suitcase lighter than when he’d arrived. Click-click-click.

Past that tree – circles of light on the ground – round – wind blowing – the circles moving – same as when he came freshman year.

Almost –

Nothing –
