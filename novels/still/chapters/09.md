# Chapter 9: Grass in the Cracks

Basic income. A fixed number. Deposited every month. Enough for rent. Enough for food. Enough for a basic AI subscription. A little left at the end of the month. A few dollars. Not enough to buy anything. But there. Every month, the last few days, he checked the balance. Still there. Like a thread. Thin. Not broken.

He got up. Washed his face. Water cold. The towel from yesterday. Unwashed. A smell. Not bad. Stale. The smell of fabric aging in dampness.

His phone. He glanced at it. No new messages. He set it down.

He ate. Spaghetti. Added an egg. The yolk in the bowl unbroken. Round. Yellow. He touched it with a fork. Broke. The yellow flowed out. Mixed with the broth. The broth went cloudy.

His phone.

He scrolled a while. Nothing registered. His thumb moving. His eyes moving. Brain not receiving. Like a window. Light coming in. But nobody in the room looking.

Dark.

He lay down. The ceiling water stain. That lake. Still there.

Grocery store. Discount chicken thighs on the bottom shelf. Tomorrow’s expiration date. The glass door had condensation. He opened it and the cold air hit his face. Like walking into another season. The chicken in clear wrap. Styrofoam tray. Price sticker. Discount. The original price crossed out with a line. Crossed out but still legible.

Next door in the cold case, salmon. Pink-orange. Pre-sliced. Black tray. The sticker: a small package. Six times the chicken’s price. He wasn’t looking at the salmon. He was looking at the chicken. But the pink-orange glow reached him from the side.

Self-checkout. Six machines. No cashier. An elderly woman ahead of him looking for the button. Her finger. Bent. Knuckle thickened. Poking the screen. The screen didn’t recognize her finger. Poked three times, no response. She put her finger to her mouth, breathed on it. Moisture. Tried again. It lit up.

“Right here.” Ethan pointed.

“Thank you, dear.” Voice thin.

Her grocery bag. Cabbage, eggs, soy sauce. Not much but heavy. When she lifted it the veins on the back of her hand stood out.

Coming out, he passed the stationery aisle. A row of pens on the shelf. Green ones.

He didn’t stop. Walked on.

Outside, the parking lot had a puddle from the morning’s rain, and in the puddle the sky was upside down — the clouds inverted, the blue deeper in the water than in the air, and at the edge of the puddle a leaf had landed with its veins facing up, the veins fanning out like the ribs of a hand, and the water was seeping into the leaf along those veins, darkening the tissue between them so that the pattern was becoming visible the way a thing becomes visible when it is ruined, the structure showing through only because the surface has been compromised, and the wind pushed a ripple across the puddle and the whole reflected sky shivered and then was still.

Grocery bag from the store to home, eight minutes. Three unmanned delivery vehicles passed on the way. White. In the bike lane, hugging the curb. Roof light blinking. No sound. Delivering what, he didn’t know. The compartments were sealed. They didn’t stop. Didn’t look at people on the sidewalk. Routes predetermined.

The street. Clean.

Streetlights lit from one end to the other. The pavement without a single crack. Last month a maintenance vehicle had come at night. Unmanned. It came while people slept. They woke and the road was smooth. Nobody saw the work being done.

Half the storefronts had changed their signs.

An unmanned convenience store door opened. Nobody went in. Door closed.

In the distance, scissors. Click click click. A barbershop. Three chairs, one occupied. A noodle shop’s sign lit up. Steam blurring the people inside the window. A fruit stand, apples glowing in the late sun. Nobody buying. The apples didn’t know.

The trash cans weren’t overflowing. The streetlights weren’t broken.

One day he walked far. From the apartment to the river. No purpose. His feet were walking.

The riverwalk. Newly paved. Colored brick. Exercise equipment along it. Twist machines, pull-up bars. Stainless steel. Brand new. Nobody using them.

The river was green. Not a clean green. Eutrophic. A film on the surface. A bird. White. Flew over. Wings not touching the water. But its shadow did.

A cleaning robot on the path. Small. White. Moving slowly. Where it passed, clean. Where it hadn’t, leaves. It didn’t pick up leaves. It swept dust. Leaves weren’t in its task parameters.

He sat on a bench. Watched the river. The river was flowing, you couldn’t tell by looking, but he dropped a leaf in and it moved leftward, and watching it move he noticed the way the current wrinkled around a rock near the bank, the wrinkles catching light in a pattern that shifted and repeated and shifted, and from the wrinkle to the light to the rock’s surface which was wet above the waterline from spray and dry below and the boundary between wet and dry was —

His phone. He looked at it. Nothing. Put it down.

He walked back.

Back at the grocery store. In the cold case, the salmon and the chicken separated by a divider. The salmon side had a light. Warm-toned. Making the pink-orange brighter. The chicken side had no light. He took the chicken. When his hand touched the plastic wrap. Cold. The cold of the case. On the other side of the divider someone took salmon. That hand’s nails were manicured.

Ethan walked over. “Let me help you.”

She looked at him. “You again.”

He scanned her items. Not much. Nineteen dollars and change. She pulled out her phone from her pocket. The case cracked at one corner. She opened the payment screen. The screen very bright. The text enlarged. She’d made the font bigger.

“Thank you, dear.”

She carried the bag out. The door opened automatically. She went through. The door closed automatically.

The park. Afternoon.

A brick path. Gaps between the bricks. Some gaps had grass. Some only dried dirt. The dirt cracked.

Where there was grass. Green. From between the bricks. Blade narrow. The veins branching from center outward –

He crouched down and looked at one. The vein starting from the base. Two branches. Splitting further. One branch reaching the blade’s edge, turning back. Where it turned back it met another branch. At the meeting point a small knot. Like two roads converging somewhere –

His phone screen lit up.

A date. He swiped it away. What date. He looked. Forgot.

Stood up. His knee cracked.

A bench. The old woman again. No phone in her hand. Nothing in her hands. Just sitting. Looking at the path ahead.

Ethan sat too. A body’s width apart. Didn’t speak.

Her hands on her knees. The veins on the backs of her hands raised. The skin had thinned. You could see the veins’ routes. From hand to fingers. Like rivers on a map.

Her nails trimmed neatly. Short. The nail surface yellowed. Old nails.

She moved. Stood up. Walked away. Very slowly. Back slightly bent.

Ethan watched her walk a stretch. She walked into the distance of the path. Gone.

Came another day. The bench. She was still there. Still no phone.

This time she spoke first. “You live around here too?”

“Yeah.”

“There used to be an auto repair shop here. By that tree. Old Frank’s. He ran it for thirty-some years. Closed two years ago.”

She didn’t say why it closed.

“Now it’s a charging station.”

She looked at the path ahead a while longer. Said nothing more.

He ran into Jake at a diner.

Six tables. Metal-shaded lamps. Warm-bulb light. Yellow. Wood grain deeper in it. On the counter, paper cranes. Red paper. Wings uneven. Seven or eight. In a row. Different sizes. Some neat, some crooked.

Jake was thinner. The fitness band on his wrist was gone. Replaced by a watch. Cheap. White face. The second hand very thin. In the lamplight like a strand of hair.

He saw Ethan. Smiled slightly. “Long time.”

Long time. The tone was right. The smile was right. But sitting down. The chair pulled out a little farther from the table than before. Not something you’d measure. A feeling. Before, two people at a diner would pull their chairs in toward the center. This time, no. The chair stayed where it was.

“Almost two years on basic income.”

“The community center has activities. Making paper cranes. Tuesdays and Thursdays. Went for a while.” He stirred his noodles with a fork. The noodles wrapping around the tines. The broth making small whirlpools.

“Stopped going.”

“Why?”

“No reason. Just stopped.” He took a bite. Chewed. Swallowed. “My hands are fine. The cranes looked fine. Just stopped going.”

Something below Ethan’s chest loosened. After loosening, it tightened.

He wanted to say something. About the messages. Those two. “Wanted to talk to you.” He opened his mouth.

“I should’ve —”

Jake looked at him. Waited.

“— called. Back then. I should’ve called.”

Jake picked up his glass. Took a sip. Set it down. The glass made a ring on the table. He looked at the ring.

“It’s fine.”

It wasn’t. They both knew. But Jake said it the way people say it when the moment for the other thing has passed and what’s left is the saying. He talked about paper cranes, basic income, community activities. Each topic was safe. Each sentence slightly longer than the Jake from before — an extra “yeah” here, “it’s fine” there. Before, Jake talked in bursts. “Hey.” “You eat?” “Whatcha watching.” Short. No distance. The distance was new. Ethan had put it there.

They finished eating. At the bottom of the bowl you could see the pattern on the ceramic. Blue. A circle. Both stood up. Jake took out his phone to scan the payment code.

“Let me get this.”

“No, no.”

Before, they didn’t say “no, no.” Before, whoever got there first paid. No ceremony.

Outside. The streetlights already on. Two people standing a moment.

“Let’s do this again sometime.” Jake said.

“Yeah.”

They walked off. Two directions.

Derek invited him out for sushi.

The restaurant was on the fourth floor of a shopping center. Inside, a server bowed. “Welcome.” Voice trained to evenness. Wood tables. Warm lighting. Screens at every seat. For ordering.

“My AI spend last month was about two grand.”

The tone he’d use for weather. Enterprise tier. A professional analysis model. An AI investment advisor, three hundred a month. Plus the basics.

Two grand. Two months of basic income. Two people at the same table. The conveyor belt running between them. Sushi on small plates spinning by. White plates, pink plates, gold plates. Color coded for price. Derek took three without looking at the color. Two pink, one gold. Ethan took one. White. He knew the color.

Derek picked up a piece of salmon. The same pink-orange as the grocery store cold case. Chewed twice. Swallowed. Reached for another. His fingers moved the way they used to move freshman year picking up boba — fast, not thinking about cost. The cost had changed. The fingers hadn’t.

The TV on. Someone speaking.

“We must learn to coexist with AI –”

He turned it off.

A forum post. “After we switched, we laid off half the team.”

Underneath: “Didn’t lay off half. Kept half. Different phrasing, same result.”

The package lockers. There used to be a delivery person outside making calls. “Hey, come grab your package.” Now the lockers received and dispensed on their own. Doors opened and closed on their own. Nobody.

One time passing by. A locker door was stuck. Half open, half closed. A package inside. Uncollected. How long, he didn’t know.

The mandatory AI sent a notification. “Good morning! It’s 54 degrees today, perfect for a walk. There’s a craft workshop nearby –”

Swiped away.

Evening. Sitting on the windowsill. Legs hanging outside. Below, the street. Streetlights on. A dog under a streetlight. No leash. Walking alone. It sniffed the ground. Walked on. Sniffed again. Its shadow three times bigger.

Thought of Marcus.

Green pen. It was green. He was sure. And when he wrote his wrist moved with each stroke, the pen touching paper at about forty-five degrees, and he didn’t lift the pen between words because he had a reluctance to break the —

Which green. There were two greens. His mother sent them. From a dollar store. They were never the right —

His face. The cheekbones. High. The mouth.

Gone. The outline was still there. The details weren’t.

His fingers curled once. Didn’t close around anything.

Nobody knew where he went.

An AI piano video. Mechanical fingers on the keys. Sound off. Watching the keys move silently. Black white black white. A finger pressed down, the key sank. Lifted, the key bounced. Every time the speed, force, arc exactly the same. Perfect. No hesitation. No key pressed too deep or too shallow.

He thought of his mother knitting the coaster – fingers in the yarn – needle in, needle out – not watching – her hands knew –

But his mother’s hands paused sometimes. When she paused he didn’t know what she was thinking. Then she continued.

The mechanical fingers didn’t pause.

His eyes burned. Closed it.
