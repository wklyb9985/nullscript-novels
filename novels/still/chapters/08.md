# Chapter 8: Eleven Days

The day he was laid off. The apartment. Door closed. His shoes still on. His keys still in his hand. Metal, cool. Held them a while, they warmed. Let go. Keys on the table. Clink.

The plastic bag at his feet. A mug, a cable, a pen. Three things. Everything six months had left. The mug had never held tea. The pen cap was still on. He tried it. Drew a line on his hand. Ink. Six months and still ink.

He’d looked at the basic income application page. Hadn’t clicked.

Outside, sounds. Cars. People. A kid yelled something. Couldn’t make it out. High-pitched. An adult voice. Lower. Then quiet.

Called home.

His phone in his hand. He looked at it a while. His mother’s name. The photo was her in the kitchen. Apron. Smiling.

Pressed call. Three rings.

His mother picked up. “What’s wrong?” Her voice a pitch higher than normal.

“How much?”

“Just a loan for now –”

“Send me the account number.”

She didn’t ask what it was for. Her voice leveled. “Did you eat?”

“Yeah.”

“What’d you eat?”

“Noodles.”

“Don’t just eat noodles. Eat some vegetables.”

“Okay.”

Hung up.

That night the money arrived. His phone buzzed once. The number was there. White background, green text.

Part of their retirement. The retirement fund was in a mutual fund. What the fund held – he didn’t think about that. He was thinking about the folding table.

He subscribed to enterprise-tier AI. Compliance tools.

First day he knew nothing.

A folding table. Bought at Walmart. Forty-nine dollars. White. Plastic. Metal tube legs. A paint bubble on one. Set it up by the window. Facing north. No direct sunlight. Outside the window, a fire escape across the alley, and below it a courtyard with a single tree whose leaves were just starting to turn. A woman on the third floor had hung laundry on the railing. The shirts moved when wind came. Ethan closed the blinds halfway.

The chair also from Walmart. White. When he sat down the legs wobbled. The floor was uneven. The back left leg short. He wedged a folded piece of paper under it. Stable.

Printouts spread across the table. Regulations, standards, screenshots. Pen marks on them. A4 paper taped to the wall. A hand-drawn flowchart. Boxes and arrows. The handwriting was his but different from college. Smaller, denser, faster.

Legal clauses nested inside each other. General provisions. Scope of application. Exceptions. Exceptions to exceptions.

The AI got the time-constraint logic backwards in the fourth nested layer. “Before this date” rendered as “after.”

Took three days to fix.

First day, tried two approaches, both wrong. The AI understood every word. But the output was off by one layer. Not logic. Word order. “Before this date” – “before” came last. The AI first locked “this date.” Then processed “before.” Correcting the direction. But the legal meaning was: “before” was the condition – the direction should be locked first, then the date. The AI couldn’t distinguish this sequence.

Second day, restructured. Better. But the fourth layer tangled again.

Third day, past midnight. The seventh approach. Each nesting layer numbered. 1.1, 1.1.1, 1.1.1.1. Each layer’s relationship to the one above locked with “belongs to,” “excludes,” “only when.” AI output. Correct.

He slapped his thigh.

Past three in the morning and his heart was beating fast, not the anxious kind but the other kind, the kind where your fingers move ahead of your thoughts and you can feel the keys warming under your hands the way they used to warm in the library freshman year when he was writing that first paper and the guitar chords came through the window and the sycamores were —

He looked at the screen. A prompt window. He’d been excited about a prompt.

Sweat on the plastic armrest of the chair. A handprint. Five finger outlines. Fingerprints. Like a signature.

First client came.

“It’ll do. Better than nothing.”

Three thousand. First payment. Not from a company payroll. Something he’d built.

Second, third, fourth. Seven clients. Enough for rent and food. Each client’s needs different. Pharmaceuticals needed terminology matching. Finance needed clause comparison. Education needed exam compliance review. Each type required recalibration. Each had its own fourth-layer nesting.

Day five, two thirty in the morning. Eyes dry. He splashed water on his face. Cold. The cold went from face to neck to chest. Woke up. Outside, the alley was quiet. A streetlight threw an orange circle on the courtyard. The tree’s shadow on the wall opposite. A cat crossed the light and was gone. He sat back down. Continued.

Day seven the chair broke. The backrest cracked. He taped it. Kept sitting.

Day nine. Chicken thighs and ramen. Chicken grease left fingerprints on the keyboard. He wiped with a napkin. Didn’t fully come off.

Fourteen hours a day. His fingers and the keys indistinguishable in temperature again.

Jake sent a message.

Not “hey.” Not “what’s up.”

“Ethan, can you look at something for me? I wrote up a plan, wanted your thoughts. No rush, whenever you have a sec.”

Then another message. Three minutes later.

“It’s not really a plan actually. I’ve just been thinking about some things. Wanted to talk to you.”

Ethan saw them. Two messages. Gray background, black text.

His finger stopped on the screen. Jake didn’t usually talk like this. “Wanted to talk to you.” He’d known Jake four years. Jake spoke in short bursts. “Hey.” “Let’s eat.” “Going back.” That kind of length. Two messages adding up to forty-something words. Forty-something words typed out by Jake’s fingers.

The fifth client’s prompt was on version three. The logic chain had broken. Starting over from the second layer.

He turned his phone face-down. Screen against the table. Dark.

Next morning he remembered. He picked up his phone. Two messages still there. Gray background, black text.

He typed “sor.” Deleted. Typed “been really.” Deleted.

He set it down.

He never replied. Not that he forgot. It was that every time he thought about replying, it was a day later. A day later it was another day later. Each additional day made the reply heavier. Heavy enough that eventually his fingers stopped.

Called home.

His father picked up.

“How’s the job?”

“Still looking.”

“Mm.”

Pause.

“Your mom sent you money.”

“Yeah.”

“Enough?”

“Yeah.”

“Mm.” Another pause. “Well – don’t overdo it.”

Hung up.

Day eleven.

Morning. He opened the laptop. Email.

Platform update notification.

“Major Update: All-New Compliance Assistant Module. Free for Enterprise Users.”

Feature list:

– Multi-layer legal clause nesting analysis. – Automatic time-constraint logic assessment. – Intelligent contract clause comparison. – One-click compliance report generation.

One click.

He read the feature list. Line by line. He recognized every line.

Multi-layer legal clause nesting analysis. Three days.

Automatic time-constraint logic assessment. Two and a half days.

Intelligent contract clause comparison. A day and a half.

One-click compliance report generation. He hadn’t gotten to that one.

Four lines. Eleven days of work. Four lines.

Clients.

Day seven, the first client: “The platform’s got this built in now. About the same.” He’d used the free tool. “About the same.” Three words.

About the same.

The last one. The education client. Very polite message. “Hey Ethan, the new platform feature is really solid. We evaluated it and we’re not going to renew for now. Thanks for everything.”

Zero. Zero.

The basic income application page. He clicked. Submitted. Approved. Fast. He typed in his name, social security, bank account. Submitted. Reviewed. Approved. Under ten seconds. Ten seconds. Identity from “entrepreneur” to “basic income recipient.”

The folding table still set up. A scratch on the surface. Pen tip pressed too hard. Went through the paper. Paper removed. The scratch more visible. A line. Left to right. Like a road. Both ends of the road, nothing.

The flowchart still on the wall. Paper corners curling. He didn’t take it down.

Derek’s SaaS lasted two years. Ethan’s lasted a few weeks. Same thing. Speed was accelerating.

Called home.

His father answered.

“How’s the job?”

“Still looking.”

“Mm.”

Home for Thanksgiving.

His mother had cooked everything he liked. Turkey. The skin golden and crackling. The juice pooling under the breast, each cut releasing a puff of steam. Stuffing from inside the bird. The bread soaked through with drippings, sage rising when she scooped it. Deviled eggs. Mashed potatoes. Green beans with almonds. Cranberry sauce. The can shape still visible. She hadn’t smoothed it. A pumpkin pie.

Nobody mentioned work. It used to be the first question. This time nobody said the word.

His father put food on his plate. “Eat up.”

His head down. Eating. Quiet.

The TV on, volume up. Not filling the silence. Filling the things not said.

His mother washed dishes. Ethan offered. She said no, rest. He stood in the kitchen doorway watching her back. The faucet running. Her back was rounder than last time. The apron tied crooked behind her. The strap hanging down, brushing her calf.

She washed. Left hand held the plate, right hand the sponge. Turned once. Set it in the rack. Next. Water sound, porcelain sound. Running water. Clink. A steady rhythm.

The dish rack filled. Plates upside down. Water beading on the bottoms. Gathering. Sliding down the curved sides. Dripping onto the rack. Drip. Drip.

She turned around. Saw him.

“What’s wrong?”

“Nothing.”

She passed him and her hand touched his arm. Wet. Cool. Dish soap foam on her fingers. White. A smear on his sleeve.

She was gone.

His arm had a spot of cool. The foam on his sleeve dried. Became a small circle.

After dinner his father sat on the couch scrolling his phone. His thumb slow. Not the fast swipes of a young person. One push at a time. Push, look, push again. Sometimes the thumb stopped. Not reading. Looking for a button.

“Can you help me with this – the font’s too small.”

He took the phone. Brightness at maximum. The case was clear but had yellowed.

A financial app. The portfolio page. He’d already opened it. Just needed to scroll.

He scrolled once. Top ten holdings. Black text on white. Third line.

He saw the name.

Not unfamiliar. That company. AI accuracy 97.3% then 99.1%. That one.

The screen brightness was too high. Allocation: 7.28%. Beside it a curve. Green. Bottom left to top right. Up. +34.7%.

He scrolled down. Fifth line. AI infrastructure. Green. +41.2%.

Eighth line. Intelligent customer service. Green.

Ten holdings, six green. Four red. Banks, real estate. Each down a few percent. The green gains larger. The green buried the red. Total return, green.

His finger stopped on the screen.

Living room TV still on. Kitchen faucet. Running. Stopped. Started again. A plate clinked.

His father sat beside him. Waiting.

“How’s it look?”

He handed the phone back.

“Good.”

His father took it. Looked once. Set it on the coffee table. On the coffee table a water ring. Left by a cup. Dried. But there.
