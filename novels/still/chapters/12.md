# Chapter 12: The Bent Hand

Woke up.

Headache. Temples throbbing. Each throb one pulse of pain. The rhythm matched his heartbeat.

Still in yesterday’s clothes. Smoke from the grill. And rain – soaked and dried – the mildew of cloth aged in dampness.

Kitchen. Faucet. Cold. His hand turned it and the metal rotated beneath his fingers – cold – water came – colder.

Cold from mouth to throat to chest.

Sat on the edge of the bed. His right hand held something.

Not something. Last night. Steadying Derek. The shoulder passing out of his hand – the palm retaining a sensation – the curve of bone – the dampness of fabric –

His hand curled once.

Whose. Last night’s. Derek’s.

Or –

Sophomore year. In the rain. A narrower shoulder – white T-shirt –

Marcus’s.

He couldn’t tell anymore. Two shoulders in the same palm. But the palm couldn’t hold the difference anymore. What it held was only – a shoulder – bone – someone’s body under his hand – and then not.

His phone. Push notification.

Dawn. Deep space probe. Launched. A thirty-eight-year project. No one on board. Three companies co-developed. Two were acquired before launch.

Went out. Work. The repair station.

On the road. Morning air cool. Last night’s rain had washed the dust – the air clearer than usual – buildings in the distance sharper. The pavement still slightly wet. His shoe soles sounded different – an added layer – thin water between shoe and road – the thud became a slap.

A large screen on the road.

“Another great leap for human civilization. Learn to coexist with AI. Together toward the stars.”

Freshman year. The poster. Marker on paper. Tilted letters.

Junior year. The lecture. A PowerPoint.

The apartment. TV.

Now. A twenty-foot screen.

Didn’t stop.

His phone lit up again.

The mandatory AI notification.

“Good morning! Let’s have a great day today too!”

Set the phone face-down on his thigh.

The screen’s warmth still in his palm.

Looked up. The sun behind clouds. The light gray-white. Diffused. Everywhere. No direction to it.

A bird called. Once. He didn’t know what kind. Not insects. A bird. One call. Short. Gone.

Waited a moment. No second call.

The repair station’s sheet-metal building ahead. The tree by the door. Leaves. Wind.

Pushed the door open. Changed into coveralls. Tools on the wall. Frank’s enamel mug on the bench. Tea steam.

“Mornin’.”

“Morning.”

Today’s orders. Three units. The last one different.

First two, routine. Warehouse robot, knee joint loose. Sorting arm, sensor drift. Tightened. Calibrated. Washed his hands.

The third.

A home care robot. White shell. Smaller than the warehouse ones. Shoulder width roughly the same as a medium-build person. Two mechanical arms, five-fingered hands at the ends – silicone skin covering the joints and sensors – they looked like hands.

The work order read: “Discomfort reported during assisted lifting mode. Shoulder force angle deviation.”

Not broken. Just uncomfortable for the user.

Ethan fixed it on the calibration stand. Opened the shoulder joint housing. Inside, the cable routing was neat – finer wires – red, yellow, blue – like blood vessels. Between the cables, soft padding – white – cushioning – he pressed it with a finger – it bounced back – like subcutaneous fat.

Shoulder joint range of motion: normal. Torque within spec. But he pressed the mechanical arm with his hand – from shoulder down to forearm – rotated – to the hand –

Five fingers. Silicone, warm – not self-generated warmth – room temperature. The surface was textured to mimic skin – fingerprints, palm lines – hard to tell from real by looking – but touching, you knew – the temperature was uniform – a human hand’s fingertips are warmer than the back of the hand – this wasn’t. At the joints he could feel metal inside. Three joints per finger. Adjustable bend radius.

He brought out the test dummy. Standard issue. Seated position. Soft body. One hundred twenty pounds. Off-white fabric. No face. The shoulder width matched a medium-build elderly person. Seated, posture unchanging. It didn’t get tired.

He ran the “assist to standing” protocol. The arm extended – the hand descended onto the dummy’s shoulder – five fingers closing – gripping –

Force was right. Angle was right. But the instant the palm came down – the finger arc was wrong. Too straight. Landing on the shoulder it felt like pressing down. Not supporting. Pressing.

Shut down the program.

Used his hand to bend the mechanical fingers. One by one.

The thumb. Inward a little. Bent until it was about like – like a person’s hand naturally coming to rest on a shoulder. Bent. Looked. A little more. The silicone was pliant – but the skeleton inside was rigid – metal – had to apply force to get the angle right – got it, released – it held. The index finger. The three middle joints slightly curved – not gripping – resting – the pad needing to contact the shoulder’s curve – first joint bent – second bent – third just barely – very slight – as though hesitating whether to touch. Middle finger. Ring finger. The pinky lightest – almost no force – but it needed to be there – needed to make contact.

All five adjusted. The mechanical hand placed on the dummy’s shoulder. Looked. Lifted it. Placed it again. The angle was off. The scapula position – the palm needed to slant slightly – from the acromion toward the back – not straight on top.

Adjusted the rotation angle. Placed it again.

This time the palm came down – five fingers closing on the shoulder – like –

Like a hand steadying a person.

Looked for a few seconds. Lifted. Placed. Again and again. Minute angles. A half-millimeter difference in finger curvature was a different feeling entirely – too tight like clutching – too loose like splaying – it had to be neither tight nor loose – a hand on a shoulder asking nothing.

He put his own hand on the dummy’s shoulder. Felt it. Palm temperature transferring from skin to fabric – beneath the fabric, stuffing – soft – no bone. His hand rested there – five fingers – naturally curled – he didn’t have to think about how to curl them. His hand knew.

Then he went to adjust the mechanical hand. One finger at a time. Using his fingers to bend it. The silicone yielding under his fingertips – the metal within the silicone yielding – two layers of material bending simultaneously – his fingers could feel two resistances – the outer soft one – the inner hard one. Adjusted until it was close. Felt his own hand again. Adjusted. Back and forth three times. The third time – it was right.

The mechanical hand on the dummy’s shoulder. His own hand on the dummy’s other shoulder. Left side, mechanical. Right side, his. Two hands. The same curvature.

The screen lit up. He pressed confirm.

1,247 units.

He pulled his hand back.

The mechanical hand stayed in that angle. Five fingers, slightly curved. Resting on the dummy’s shoulder. The silicone under the fluorescent light had a faint sheen.

He looked at it a while. Curved. Five. Resting on the shoulder. The silicone color wasn’t quite human – a shade too light – slightly gray – but in this light – without looking carefully – it looked like a hand.

Frank stood at the door. Had been standing there – Ethan didn’t know how long. Didn’t speak. Looked once. Turned to pour his tea.

Washed his hands. Steel wool. Cold water. Iron taste. The grease in his palm lines washed off one layer. Under the nails still dark. Water flowing between his fingers. Cold.
