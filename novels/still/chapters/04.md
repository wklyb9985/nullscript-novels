# Chapter 4: The Eight Percent

The train back to campus was in early September, and the light through the window was different from when he’d left — thinner, the green of the fields less saturated, leaning already toward yellow at the edges. He looked out the window and tried to watch the way he used to watch, the power poles passing, the wire sagging between them, but his hand found his phone and his thumb moved and the fields slid by unnoticed.

Junior year. September. The dorm door was the same door.

The smell had changed. Two summer months of vacancy. Dust had settled over everything. He pushed the window open. Wind came in. The dust smell thinned. A layer of dust on the desk surface. His finger drew a line. A streak. The desk’s original color.

His pillow from home. His mother had washed it. The scent fainter than freshman year. Washed too many times. Each wash it faded a layer. Now only a trace. Had to press his nose to it.

Professor Harris’s class. First day. Eighteen people. Last semester sixty-three, then eighteen. This semester still eighteen. The same eighteen. The room felt wider.

On the board he wrote: Discount rate. 8%.

“Why eight.”

Nobody answered.

“Where does the eight come from?”

He answered himself: “The model calculated it. Twenty years of data. It says eight. We use eight.”

Paused. Looked at them. His finger drew a circle in the air. The circle was tighter than last year’s circles.

“What happens when you use a number you didn’t arrive at yourself.”

Nobody.

“You use it and it works. You keep using it. After a while you forget there was a time before the number. The number becomes the floor. Everything else stands on it.”

After class. Hallway. A girl leaning on the wall waiting for somebody. Water bottle in her hand. Not drinking. The cap turning – loosened a little – tightened back – loosened – tightened. The person she was waiting for hadn’t come.

Ethan passed her and caught something – not perfume – laundry detergent – didn’t know the brand – smelled it and that was that.

His mouth was dry.

The campus recruiting fair was the second week of October. A gym turned into rows of tables. Each table had a banner, a stack of brochures, a person or two behind a tablecloth.

Ethan went with Chris.

Chris moved fast. Table to table. Took a brochure, glanced, moved on. His dimples visible when he smiled at the recruiter – “Thanks, thanks” – three tables a minute.

Ethan moved slower. One table had a woman in a blazer, her nails tapping the tablecloth – fast – like typing without a keyboard. Behind her, a pull-up banner: “Join the Future of Human-Machine Collaboration.” Below it, a salary range. The top number had a comma in it. Eighty thousand.

Next table. Another banner. “Content Analysis – Entry Level.” Salary range: thirty-five hundred a month.

Eighty thousand a year. Thirty-five hundred a month. Three tables apart. The same gym. The same fluorescent lights overhead.

He picked up a brochure from the thirty-five-hundred table. The paper was thinner than the eighty-thousand table’s. He could feel the difference between his thumb and finger.

“AI assistance has reduced workload,” the recruiter said. “Compensation is market-adjusted accordingly.”

“Accordingly” – he stood there holding the brochure. The recruiter had already turned to the next person.

Walked on. Chris was gone – somewhere ahead – his round head visible above the crowd for an instant, then not.

At the end of the row, a table with no one sitting behind it. A few brochures left in a stack, fanned out. No banner. Just a printed sign taped to the front: “Positions Filled.” The tape on the left corner had come loose. The sign hung at an angle.

He stood in front of it longer than any other table.

The interview was in November. Video. A content review company.

His laptop on the desk. Webcam – the small light beside it – green – on. He’d put on a button-down. Below the frame: sweatpants. The collar stiff – new – hadn’t been washed – the fold-crease still visible on the shoulder.

First ten minutes, normal. His background. His coursework. His GPA. Numbers. The interviewer typed while he spoke – the keyboard sound coming through thin – distant – she wasn’t looking at him, she was looking at her screen.

The last question.

“What can you bring that AI can’t provide?”

Three seconds.

In three seconds – “creativity” “critical thinking” “empathy” “communication” – each one surfacing and vetoing itself before it reached his mouth. Each word had been in a brochure somewhere in that gym.

Three seconds passed. The interviewer waited.

“I’m – pretty reliable.”

The webcam light turned off. Green to dark.

After. Hallway. The wood floor. His feet, the old creaking. Past the bulletin board – the glass cover had a new sheet behind it.

“The following fields have experienced significant shifts in employment outlook: Accounting, Translation, Entry-Level Software Development, Administrative Management, Basic Legal Services –”

Ethan’s major wasn’t on it. He felt relief. Jake’s was.

Fingerprints on the glass – someone’s – pressed over the word “Translation” – the word still visible beneath them.

That night.

Couldn’t sleep. Upper bunk. Ceiling. The dead mosquito. Still there. Wings spread.

Below him, Jake’s bunk. A sound. The phone screen’s light – blue-white, blue-white – on the ceiling. Jake scrolling. Thumb moving up. Slow. Not fast swipes. One push at a time.

Then a sound. Very quiet. Not turning over. Not breathing. His nose drawing in – short – a sniff – then another – the intervals irregular.

He was crying. Or not crying. His nose was sniffing. Mouth closed. Only his nose. A sniff. Another. As if drawing something in – not letting it come out.

The phone was still lit. His hand still scrolling. The light jumped. But the nose kept sniffing.

About two minutes. Then quiet. His phone went dark.

Ethan wanted to say something. His mind produced – “the job market is cyclical” “find your niche in a vertical” “learn to coexist with AI” “find the optimal point of human-machine collaboration” – stopped. Every sentence was familiar. Every sentence was someone else’s.

Closed his eyes.

The next day Jake’s eyes weren’t swollen. He’d washed his face. Went to the dining hall normally. Ate normally.

He asked Ethan if he’d look at his resume. Ethan said sure. Jake said he’d send it later.

He never sent it. Ethan never asked.

Derek’s social media. A photo. Two people shaking hands. Company logo behind them.

“Sold the project.”

A row of likes. One from Professor Harris.

Ethan turned off his phone. Set it face-down on the desk. On the desk, dust in the corner where the wall met the surface. His finger traced the edge but didn’t wipe it.

A notebook. His notebook. He’d brought it to the library to work on the paper.

Opened it. Flipped to the middle. Found the page from freshman year. The ink dot was still there. Where the pen had touched and he’d hesitated – and the ink seeped out – a small circle. Beside it, the half-sentence he’d crossed out, the other sentence he’d kept. Between the two ink dots, words. A path a thought had traveled.

He looked at the ink dot. The fibers around it had yellowed slightly. The dot was still dark.

Pen in hand. Tip on a blank page.

Nothing.

Set the pen down. Closed the notebook.
