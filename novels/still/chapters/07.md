# Chapter 7: Indemnified

99.1%. From 97.3 to 99.1.

That afternoon. A report.

Same as the others. But he stopped.

It wasn’t that he saw something. It was a feeling. His finger hovering over the mouse. The mouse shell beneath his fingertip. A faint seam. The mold line. His finger resting on that seam.

Ten minutes. His fingertip was sweating. The office. Air conditioning hum, keyboards, someone coughed. Ten minutes. Everyone else had signed twenty reports. His screen still showed the one.

Zoomed in. Read it line by line. Every number. Every unit. Page three, a paragraph on trend analysis. AI-written. Smooth. Logical. But his hand didn’t want to click Approve.

Read it three times.

No error.

Clicked Reject. In the reason field: “Recommend secondary verification of data sources.”

Next day. His manager: “The re-review came back. AI was correct. Please be more careful going forward. A rejection triggers a process review. It affects the efficiency metrics.”

He never rejected again.

Tom. The desk beside his. That morning he’d brought in a small plant — plastic — set it by his monitor. The leaves were dusty. He wiped one with his thumb. His eleven o’clock stretch came and went. The chair creaked.

He’d signed one of three hundred that day. The same as the other two hundred ninety-nine.

Something went wrong.

Company statement: “Following human review and approval, the responsible reviewer has been suspended.”

Ethan helped him carry his box to the elevator. The box wasn’t heavy. A mug, some cables, a notebook, a pen. The same kind of pen. Same arc of the signature.

Tom took it. His hand touched Ethan’s fingers. An instant. Fingertip to fingertip. His hand was colder than Ethan’s.

As the door was closing he opened his mouth and started to say something about the sparkling water, about the eleven o’clock stretch, about the way Tom’s chair creaked at a pitch that had become part of the room’s —

No sound.

The door closed.

That afternoon nobody sat at the workstation. The sticker on the chairback had been peeled off, leaving a white mark. On the desk, a paper coaster. Round. A water ring. His cup had left it. The fluorescent tube above Tom’s station buzzed differently now. Not louder. A different pitch. The air conditioning cycled on, cycled off. Somewhere in the building, a door closed. Next day someone new was in the chair. The coaster was still there. The new person didn’t remove it.

AI Output Liability Insurance. The review department had thirty people. Personnel cost: three hundred thousand a year. Insurance premium: eighty thousand.

Eighty thousand versus three hundred thousand. Thirty bodies in chairs. Pens in hands. The arc of the signature. All replaced by a line of numbers.

HR called Ethan in.

Same office. Frosted glass. Pleather armrests that stuck to his forearms in the heat. The chair was lower than the desk, so he looked up at her. On the wall behind her, a clock. The second hand moved in small jerks, not smooth. Each jerk a click he could feel in his teeth.

“Last time I told you the position was basically an insurance policy. Now there’s an actual insurance policy.”

N+1 severance. He signed. Same arc as the reviews. Wrist still. Fingers moved. His hand was shaking slightly. Not fear. The pen was heavier than the one at his workstation.

The last page: “Retroactive release of liability.”

Signed.

Packed up his desk. A coffee mug, never used. A charging cable. A pen.

He turned the mug upside down and looked. The bottom was dry. No water ring. The cable and the pen slid into a plastic bag, touching with a faint sound. His finger hooked the handle. The plastic edge dug into the second joint of his index finger.

Elevator. 6. 5. 4.

He thought of Tom. Same spot.

Walked out the front door. A little past four in the afternoon. The plastic bag was very light. The wind made it sway.

The flower bed by the entrance. Red flowers. Alive. The sprinkler system was watering them. Automatic. The mist even. Nobody was watering them.

His phone lit up. Push notification. “Company X announces operational restructuring, reducing labor costs. After-hours stock price up 1.2%.”
